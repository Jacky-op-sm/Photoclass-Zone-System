Appendix V
Examples: Zone System Applications

The idea for this appendix was inspired by my favorite Ansel Adams book, Examples: The Making of 40 Photographs. In this classic, Ansel describes in great detail, and with thoughtful autobiographical comments, how he created many of his greatest images. His book allows you to begin to understand the complexities of his creative process and the role the Zone System played in making his photography masterful.

Contrary to some misconceptions, the Zone System is an extremely versatile tool with applications in many areas of photographic practice. This appendix demonstrates how you can apply the Zone System to color, architectural, digital, studio, figurative, and documentary photography.

Six participating photographers were asked to describe in their own words how the Zone System contributed to the creation of their images. These statements represent very personal approaches and illustrate how, with experience, you can adapt the Zone System to a wide range of photographic problems.

The common denominator in all these examples is previsualization. The ability to use the Zone Scale to form a mental image of your final print before taking the picture is a powerful creative advantage. After the image is previsualized, the problem of how to achieve the desired result depends on the nature of the shooting circumstances and your materials.

My example of photographing with portable strobes on a street corner in Oakland California demonstrates the Zone System’s flexibility.

I would like to thank Christine Alicino, David Bayles, Dan Burkholder, Judy Dater, and Robert Bruce Langham III and the family of Wynn Bullock for their generosity and cooperation in preparing these examples.

Christine Alicino

This portrait was done for Rolf Engle’s 60th birthday so I was looking for an image of him that was stately, sensitive, and conveyed his dignity and creativity.

Rolf Engle, 2002.

What I love about working with lights in the studio is that I can control the technical aspects of the process so that I can concentrate on the mood and presence of the subject.

This was especially important in this case because I was using Polaroid Type 55 film, which meant that the exposure and contrast needed to be perfect since there was no opportunity to change things after the fact.

I previsualized this portrait as having soft but dramatic contrast. The background needed to be dark but light enough to allow for separation with his hair (Zones III and IV). His skin needed to be luminous but detailed (Zones VI and VII).

Since the light source was a continuous, daylight balanced light with a Fresnel lens in a soft box, I used the distance between the subject and the background to control the shadow values and between the subject and the light source to control the highlights. I used an incident light meter to determine the exposure.

Christine Alicino is a freelance professional and fine art photographer who shoots people, products, editorial, and environmental photography in the San Francisco Bay Area (www.christinealicino.com).

David Bayles

This photograph was made with a view camera in the early 1970s, under the influence of reading John Cage and of wondering about randomness. I deliberately sought randomly organized objects as subjects—among them scatterings of driftwood imbedded in wind-smoothed sand (see Figure 4.2) and jumbles of fallen flowers and leaves.

Untitled, 1970s.

But by November, fallen leaves are drab. The negative was developed about N + 2; even so, there was a need for considerable burning of the darker values and some holding back of the lighter ones, all on fairly contrasty paper.

There is a kind of picture that glows even though the range of tones is quite compact, deep, and relatively soft. While such pictures may be hard to print, the tonalities aren’t really hard to previsualize. The harder part is the anticipation of what—if anything—is the real content.

David Bayles studied photography with Ansel Adams and, together with Ted Orland, is the author of Art and Fear: Observations On the Perils (and Rewards) of Artmaking. David’s work has been widely exhibited. He lives and works in Eugene, Oregon.

Dan Burkholder

125th Street Station, New York City.

HDR in the Landscape

Looking over the way different people use the Zone System, I’m reminded of the fable of the blind men examining the elephant: The man feeling the elephant’s leg says, “an elephant is like a tree,” another man feeling the ear says, “an elephant is like a leaf” and on it goes. The range of Zone System interpretations really isn’t very different.

There was a time in my own image making when I used the Zone System more traditionally. That was way back when I used a view camera and spot meter to place my shadows and measure highlights to determine times for film development. Times have changed for me and the way I make photographs. More than 12 years have passed since I exposed a sheet (or roll, or cassette, or disk—yes, at one time we had “disk” cameras, and I don’t mean computer disks but round disks of film). Heck, at the moment, my camera of choice is an iPhone! These iPhone images are printed either as archival inkjet prints or as hand-coated platinum/palladium prints on a thin vellum paper behind which I apply 24K gold leaf. What hasn’t changed over the decades is the need to make tonal decisions. Whereas we used to bend the tonal response of our materials chemically, we now do it by manipulating numbers in an image file.

Plant on Church Floor, New Orleans.

HDR is simply the digital way of dealing with issues of high dynamic range in the scene. Instead of varying our exposure and film development, we supply several bracketed exposures that computer software (or iPhone apps!) can meld together, producing a final image with lush detail in all regions of the image.

It’s important to emphasize that our 2011 HDR methods are merely a temporary fix—a somewhat klutzy workaround necessitated by our current breed of image sensors that don’t have the dynamic range we want and need. But, fear not, in just a few years we’ll have more dynamic range than we know what to do with. Whether it’s because of sensor improvements or cameras that nearly instantaneously combine that bracketed series of captures, the days of manually shooting bracketed images and using special software will soon be behind us. But that’s in the future. In the mean time, to paraphrase a former defense secretary, we go to HDR photography with the version of the sensor we have, not the version we’d like to have.

Judy Dater

This photograph was taken very late in the afternoon after the sun had set. I was attracted to the strange and inexplicable white boulder sitting on the dark red-brown earth. I wanted to heighten the contrast of the white rock, rope, and figure against the dark ground. I metered the earth and placed it on Zone III. Then I metered the rock and found that I would have to develop the film at N + 2 to bring it up to Zone VII. This produced the desired effect.

Untitled, 1983.

Chris Johnson

Tyrone Burns

This portrait was done for the Oakland Fence Project, an interactive community arts project devoted to honoring local heroes in Oakland neighborhoods. I shot this in the late afternoon using a Phase One P40+ digital back and portable strobe units with four heads to light Tyrone and the barbershop behind him.

Tyrone Burns, Owner—Pull Up Your Pants Barbershop, 2016

In Chapter 11, “The Zone System and Studio Photography,” I explain how, after you determine your Key Aperture that provides the depth of field you need for a given image, you can use incident flash meter aperture readings to produce the lighting effects you need.

For example, the readings for his face were f/11 for the key light on the bright side of his face and f/8 for the darker side. The two strobes lighting the building behind him were set to watt second powers and distances that resulted in readings of f/8. (Review Chapter 11 if this is not clear.)

An important aesthetic consideration when shooting outdoors with strobes is how your choice of shutter speeds affects the ambient light. A normal exposure for this scene would have been f/11 at 1/30. Instead I used f/11 at 1/60 to darken the overall ambient lighting.

Another exciting element of this project is that I created a short video of Tyrone explaining why he calls his barbershop Pull Up Your Pants.

  1. Go to the Apple App Store and download the Oakland Fence Project mobile app to your iPhone.
  2. Once the app loads, open it and dismiss the Welcome Screen.
  3. Aim the camera frame at my portrait of Tyrone on these pages and the video will automatically begin to play. This effect is called augmented reality art.

Robert Bruce Langham III

I was up against the east wall of the volcanic neck, just above a popular pull-up spot. Very rugged to move about. I could see the weather change coming from the west. My meter was a Pentax Spot with a Zone VI scale pasted on the dial. I kept checking the contrast between the big shadow and the sunlit area. Didn’t want to lose either, and they never threatened the range of the film beyond what you might expect. If forced to choose, I was going to give up shadow to get highlights. The light changed rapidly in quantity out on the plains while the shadow held steady. Never got contrasty beyond reason.

I started shooting my few sheets of film wondering how much better or worse it was going to get. Exposures ran around 1/2 second at 22 with a polarizer. Driving rainstorm ran me into the truck as the light turned flat. I sat and reloaded my holders anticipating better light after it lifted, but it just got dark.

East Dike, Summer Storm, Shiprock, New Mexico, 2004.

Robert Bruce Langham III is a photographer and writer who lives and works very well, right where he was born in Tyler, East Texas.

Wynn Bullock

This remarkable image has been known as “Let There Be Light” since 1955 when Edward Steichen paired it with the famous biblical quote for his seminal “The Family of Man” exhibition at the Museum of Modern Art in New York. When this exhibition opened at the Corcoran Gallery in Washington, DC, “Let There Be Light” was voted the most popular image in the show.

At first glance most people assume that this image depicts moonlight over the ocean at night but, in fact, Wynn Bullock used an 8 x 10 view camera to make this exposure as the sun was setting over a fully lit beach in Big Sur, California.

The dramatic difference between Bullock’s creative vision of this moment, and the way it would have looked in a normal rendering is a perfect example of applied photographic technique. The results are inspiring!

Here is the way that Wynn Bullock has written about the way he photographed:

From the beginning I have placed great emphasis on the fact that darkroom work is an important element in the creative photographic process. When photographing I become so involved in what I’m perceiving that I have to force myself to think of technique. I use a Weston meter, take a high and low reading, and bracket my exposures. My negatives aren’t always what they should be, but that’s the way it is.

Wynn Bullock was a master printer and heavily manipulated his prints in the darkroom. The basic exposure used to create “Let There Be Light” would normally be considered drastically underexposed were it not for the fact that achieving a powerful contrast between the deep blacks of the beach and the luminous stream of water were clearly his intentions.

Wynn Bullock (April 18, 1902–November 16, 1975) is one of the master photographers of the twentieth century whose work is included in over 90 major museum collections around the world. During his lifetime he published numerous books and is acclaimed in all of the standard histories of modern photography. In addition to numerous bodies of complex and beautiful work, Bullock also thought deeply about the nature of human perception, the underlying nature of reality and human perception.
