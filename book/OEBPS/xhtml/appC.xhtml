Appendix C
What Is a Pixel?

Pixel Size

The easiest way to confuse beginning digital photographers would be to ask them with a straight face, “What size is a pixel?”

The reason this is a trick question is that the size of a “pixel” entirely depends on the kind of pixel it happens to be. For example, every person in a football stadium card stunt is essentially a pixel. This is why the definitions of pixels and resolution can be so confusing.

The word “resolution” refers to the number of pixels there are per inch of a given imaging media. In terms of size, pixels come in two different types: fixed hardware pixels and variable image pixels.

Screen Pixels

On the surface of a computer monitor, a pixel is a fixed and rigid physical entity that can never change size. The resolution of monitors is set to values such as 72 or 264 pixels per inch, and that always remains the same.

But keep in mind that these “monitor pixels” are only the tiny dots that create the “image pixels” you see on the screen.

When the screen displays a digital image, these “screen pixels” are used to create image pixels that can be almost any size. For example, when one of these screen pixels is used to reproduce one image pixel, the image display in Photoshop reads 100 percent, or “Actual size.” When two screen pixels are used to display one image pixel, the image shrinks to 50 percent.

Digital Image Sensor Pixels

The size of pixels on the surface of a digital image sensor inside a camera is also rigidly fixed.

In a 6-megapixel camera chip, there are just over 6 million pixels on the chips surface, and they can’t change size.

These fixed sensor pixels store the visual information that computers use to create digital images.

The amount of information recorded by a 6-megapixel sensor is enough to create an approximately 9″ × 12″ image that prints with 240 dots per inch on an inkjet printer. In terms of digital image quality, this resolution number is the one that means the most.

Scanners

Scanner manufacturers represent the fixed, optical resolution of their products with two numbers: 1200 × 2400 dots per inch (dpi), for example. In this case, 1200 is the number of sensors that are positioned horizontally in the scanner head, and 2400 is the number of steps the carriage motor can take as it travels one inch down the film or document being scanned.

When scanner manufacturers claim that their scanner has a maximum resolution of 9600 dpi, they are describing the amount of information the scanner software can interpolate, or fake.

Digital image pixels that are created by scanners can be many different sizes depending on how many slices, or “samples,” the scanner divides the image into. For example, a 300 sample per inch (spi) pixel is 1/300th of an inch wide. A 1 spi pixel is one full inch wide!

Scanners usually refer to the image pixels they create as dots per inch or dpi because, just like camera sensors, the scanner is recording visual information that is intended to be rendered as dots of pigment on the surface of photographs created by an inkjet printer.

One other measure of a scanner’s quality is its density range. This number describes the amount of contrast the scanner can record; the higher this number, the greater the scanner’s ability to render detail in the shadow and highlight areas. Density range numbers above 3.2 are very good.

Printers

The dots per inch that we see as image pixels in inkjet prints are actually made from extremely small clouds of pigment deposited on the paper by the physical holes in the print head of the printer. For example, there may be 1440 of these tiny holes in the printer head that spread ink on paper to create the image pixels we see in the print.
