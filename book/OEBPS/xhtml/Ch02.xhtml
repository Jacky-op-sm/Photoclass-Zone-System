Print Quality, Negative Contrast, and Dynamic Range

Let’s begin by defining a few important terms. Throughout this book you will find that the word contrast has different meanings depending on whether I am referring to the contrast of the subject you are photographing or the negative or digital file that you will use to make the print. In general, the word “contrast” refers to the relative difference between dark and light areas of the subject or negative or in your digital image.

Subject contrast refers to the difference between the amounts of light reflected by the darker, or “shadow,” areas of the scene and the lighter, or “highlight,” areas (a dark door as opposed to a white wall, for example).

[note] In the Zone System, the words “shadow” and “highlight” are often used as general terms to describe any darker or lighter areas of the scene. Areas of the scene that reflect more or less light are also called “values.” Thus, a white wall is said to be a “highlight value,” and a dark wall could be called a “shadow value.” In the digital realm we use the term “tonal levels” when referring to lighter or darker tones in the subject or image file. These terms will become more familiar as we go along.

Negative contrast refers to the relative difference between the more transparent areas of the negative and those that are more opaque. Because photographic negatives are actually coated with extremely thin layers of silver, they are said to have shadow densities and highlight densities.*

The shadow densities are more transparent and correspond to the darker parts of the print.

The highlight densities are more dense in the negative and appear as the lighter areas of the print. If any of this is unclear, refer to the section “Photographic Emulsions” in Appendix G (page 264) before going on.

Dynamic range is the term used in digital photography when referring to the difference between the darkest and lightest tonal values or levels in an image. You can see an image’s dynamic range as bright and dark tones in your camera preview or monitor, and graphically illustrated as histograms in some of Adobe Photoshop’s image editing tools. (See Chapter 10, “The Zone System and Digital Photography,” for illustrations of these tools.)

Subject Contrast and Photographic Papers: A Bit of History

It is easy to visualize how dramatically the contrast of photographic subjects can vary from one situation to another. Imagine the difference between photographing a dark tree surrounded by brightly lit snow on one day and then shooting a portrait of a blond woman wearing a light dress on a beach when it’s cloudy. One scene would be extremely contrasty and the other gray and flat.

The relative contrast of a given scene depends on how light or dark the objects in the picture are and on how much light is falling on them. Learning how to adapt your shooting and film-developing methods to suit a variety of different lighting situations is essential because modern photographic films and papers produce unsatisfactory results when the contrast of the scene is either too great or too flat. Similar problems apply to digital shooting, as you will soon see.

It used to be that photographic papers were much more forgiving of negatives with excessive contrast. It’s fascinating to look at old glass plate negatives and notice how amazingly dense and contrasty they could be. The reason is that legacy photographic papers were formulated with emulsions that were much less sensitive to light when compared to contemporary papers. It wasn’t unusual for exposures to be many minutes under bright sunlight. This, of course, meant that the images couldn’t be enlarged, so the large plate negatives were instead contact printed in glass frames. The trade-off was that, in exchange for the process being slow, photographers were able to beautifully print negatives that had an amazingly wide range of contrasts.

Modern photographic papers are very different. To allow paper emulsions to record short exposures under the artificial lights of enlargers, contemporary papers can now print negatives with only a relatively narrow range of contrasts.

The Photographic Procrustean Beds

In ancient Greece there was a myth about a diabolical innkeeper named Procrustes who offered passing strangers an invitation to spend the night on his special iron bed which he claimed would magically fit all guests. What he didn’t reveal was that to enforce his “one size fits all” policy, he would either stretch unsuspecting short visitors on a rack until they fit his bed or cut off lengths of their legs if they happened to be too tall.

Historic photographic papers could generously accommodate a wide range of negative contrasts, but modern papers are distinctly “procrustean,” because photographers now have to either expand or compress the contrast of their negatives so that they print well on the paper of their choice.

In the digital world, this very same problem is described in terms that are ironically also very procrustean. Under- or overexposed high-contrast digital image files are said to have highlight and shadow tonal levels that are “clipped,” meaning that they are literally cut off by the editing software and are lost forever if you try to print them.

Working with Paper Grades

Every beginning photographer has attempted to make a beautiful print from a negative that has more or less than average contrast. Typically, you find yourself wasting many sheets of paper as you switch to higher contrast papers or filters to compensate for negatives that print too gray; or lower grades for negatives that are too contrasty. Either way, the results are usually disappointing. Some photographers even assume that this is what paper grades are essentially designed for.

A better procedure would be to understand the tonal separation qualities of each grade of paper and choose one that best suits the aesthetic qualities of your work. For example, some photographers like Imogen Cunningham prefer the softer tonal gradations of low-contrast papers. Others, like W. Eugene Smith, prefer the harder, more dramatic tonal transitions of higher contrast papers.

Ideally, what you would do is choose a grade of paper that best suits your work and establish that as your standard. You may, for example, decide for aesthetic reasons that from now on grade or multicontrast filter #3 will be your normal grade of paper. With this established, your goal would be to learn how to make “perfect negatives”; in other words, ones whose contrasts would allow you to easily print them on your grade 3 regardless of how contrasty or flat the original scene happened to be.

If the contrast of photographic subjects never changed, as in a studio where the lighting can be controlled, it would be easy to adopt a standard shooting and processing method that would give you good results every time. Because the contrast of scenes in the real world can differ greatly, it is necessary to learn first how to measure subject contrast and then how to compensate for it.

Although there are no films or chemical developers in digital photography, the exposure and contrast problems discussed and illustrated in the following section are very important for digital photographers to understand. Unless you use certain limited techniques that are discussed in Chapter 10, there is no way to recover highlight detail that has been obliterated because of excessive contrast in your subject and overexposure. Even if you never intend to work with film, I suggest that you carefully read through this and the following sections so that the Zone System is conceptually clear to you. We will be applying many of these concepts to the digital world, as you will see in forthcoming chapters. Along the way in this text, I will make reference to specific issues that apply to digital photography.

Working with Problem Negatives

Let’s consider the four basic problems that photographers encounter when trying to make a good print from a negative that has been improperly exposed and developed.

As you will see, these four problems fall into two categories: solvable problems and those that are fatal to achieving fine prints.

The Solvable Problems

Overexposure and underdevelopment are serious problems that are more or less correctable in the printing process. In digital photography, this corresponds to working with an image that is too gray and flat. You’d like to avoid this, but these problems are manageable in Photoshop.

The Fatal Problems

I realize that calling these problems fatal is a dramatic way to express this point, but the fact is that underexposure and overdevelopment are essentially lethal negative flaws that are uncorrectable. One great advantage of the Zone System is that it is specifically designed to help you avoid these two problems.

Although digital images are never “developed” in the chemical sense that film is, it is still possible to end up with an image that has problems very similar to the ones described next.

Underexposure

The simplest way to define a “good exposure” is to say that it means choosing a combination of f/stop and shutter speed that will allow the right amount of light to expose the film or digital sensor. It is important to understand that if the film or sensor receives less than this optimum amount of exposure, the negative will be too thin in the areas that correspond to the darker parts of the subject. For digital image files, underexposure results in both empty dark areas and excess noise. What makes proper exposure for either film or digital photography so crucial is that the only time your camera can record visual information in the darker shadow areas of your subject is during exposure.

Of course, underexposure will cause the entire negative to be less dense than normal, but the lack of density in the shadow areas of the negative is critical. When a negative receives too little exposure, it will be transparent in areas that you would ordinarily expect to print with full texture and detail (dark hair and fabric, for example). Because the necessary detail is not in the negative or file, these areas print as black, empty spaces.

Deliberate underexposure can often be used to create striking effects, but in general, these unnaturally dark areas are distracting and spoil the quality of your image. Of course, any dark area of a print can be made lighter by dodging when you make a print, but no amount of careful printing can give a print detail that doesn’t exist in the negative.

As you will learn in the Chapter 10, underexposure is just as damaging to digital image files as it is to film. For this reason, avoiding underexposure is essential. The rule is: A negative or digital file has to receive at least enough exposure to ensure that the important darker areas of the print appear realistically well lit and detailed.

Overexposure

Overexposure results in negatives that are too dense in the important shadow areas of the print. Prints made from negatives or digital files that are overexposed will be gray and lack richness and depth.

Overexposed negatives are often excessively grainy with shadow areas that are too light, but it’s possible to correct for this problem to some extent by giving your print more exposure under the enlarger.

Underdevelopment

Film development has its main effect on the denser or highlight areas of a negative. An underdeveloped negative will be too thin in these areas, and the resulting print generally will be too dark with no sense of light or brilliance.

Underdeveloped negatives usually print flat on normal grades of paper, but using a grade or filter higher than your normal one can compensate for this to some extent.

Although there is no development phase in the digital process, a low-contrast flat, digital image looks pretty much the same as a print from an underdeveloped negative.

Overdevelopment

If a negative is overdeveloped, either from being developed for too long or in a developing solution that was too concentrated or too hot, or from being agitated too aggressively, the highlight areas will be too dense.

Because these densities are relatively opaque, the corresponding light areas of the print will be too white, or “blocked up.” Burning in these areas when you make a print will make them darker, but it cannot replace the lost texture and detail.

Although there are no films or chemical developers in digital photography, blown-out highlight areas are just as serious a problem. As I mentioned earlier, subject areas that are too bright are clipped by the editing software, and there is no way to recover the detail that was in those areas.

A print from a properly exposed and developed negative will have rich, detailed shadow areas and brilliant, textured whites.

Summary

So far, you have learned that, although film and digital photography differ in many ways, they are both “procrustean” in that you have to adapt the way you shoot and process the negative or digital file so that important subject values fit within the parameters of the medium you’ve chosen.

  1. If you are working with film, after you have, for aesthetic reasons, decided on your optimum, normal paper grade, learning how to control the contrast of your negatives is necessary because of the range of subject contrasts photographers have to cope with.

    If you are working with digital image files, it is also critical that you make sure that your subject’s shadow and highlight values are not clipped.

  2. With film, until you learn how to adapt your methods of exposure and development to suit a variety of lighting situations, you will only produce easily printable negatives when the contrast of your subject happens to be average.

    The principles of exposure also apply to shooting with digital cameras, but the methods for correcting contrast and image quality problems are different. They will be covered in Chapter 10. Understanding these principles is the first key to becoming a better photographer.

  3. With film, exposure has its primary effect on the shadow densities of the negative. Film development primarily affects the highlight densities.
  4. Any combination of over- or underexposure or development will result in a negative that is either too dense or too thin.
  5. The two “fatal flaws” of film photography are underexposure and overdevelopment because they damage your negative quality in ways that cannot be compensated for when you make a print. Severe underexposure permanently obliterates necessary detail in the negative’s shadow areas. Overdevelopment destroys detail in the highlight areas of the negative.

The corresponding problems in digital photography are underexposure, which results in dark and noisy shadow areas, and overexposure, which results in clipped highlights that print as empty whites.

Trying to make a fine print from a negative or digital file with contrast problems can be a costly and frustrating ordeal. With film, to a certain extent, graded or variable contrast papers can help, but learning to control the contrast of the negative itself through proper exposure and development is the secret to consistently better results.

Now that we have defined the problems, let’s begin to approach the solution. Although the next chapter uses examples drawn from film photography, I highly recommend that digital photographers read through and learn the principles explained. As you will see, this will make it much easier for you to understand how the Zone System works in the digital realm.
