Appendix B
A Primer on Studio Lighting Photography

Controlling Strobe Brightness in the Studio

Studio strobes provide you with two basic ways to increase or decrease the amount of light illuminating photographic subjects in a studio: You can adjust the distance between the flash head and the subject, or you can increase or decrease the output of the strobe unit within this limits of its capacity. This appendix explains the basics of controlling flash output with strobe power pack adjustments. (See Chapter 11 for a detailed explanation of the effect of distance on exposure in light studios.)

The power packs of modern studio strobe units are capacitors, meaning that they function in the opposite way to batteries. A battery’s efficiency depends on its ability to hold energy and dispense it very slowly.

A capacitor is just the opposite. Strobe power packs draw energy from the outlet or batteries they are plugged into and, when they are ready to discharge, the Ready/Test light glows (#1), as shown in Figure B.1.

Figure B.1 Typical strobe power pack configuration.

In the diagrammed power pack, notice the three ports that allow you to attach three flash heads to this pack (2).

The total amount of energy in the power pack is measured in units known as “watt-seconds” that relate to exposure in a very logical and simple way:

  • All power packs provide ways of increasing and decreasing the amount of energy that’s distributed to a single head. (See the manual for how the step-down switches are designed for the pack you are using.)
  • When the amount of watt-seconds distributed to a single strobe head is doubled, the exposure at a given distance increases by one stop, or one Zone.
  • When the amount of watt-seconds is reduced by one-half, the exposure at a given distance decreases by one stop, or one Zone.

The total number of flash heads plugged into a power pack also influences the output of each head attached.

For this example, let’s assume that the total capacity of a pack is 2000 watt-seconds and that all this energy is being distributed to the single flash head that is attached to the pack.

Figure B.2 Strobe power pack with one head attached.

The total amount of watt-seconds is always divided by the number of strobe heads plugged into the power pack.

For example, if the total amount of watt-seconds in a given power pack is 2000, with one strobe head plugged in, all 2000 watt-seconds would go to that head, and the exposure might be f/22 at a given distance.

If another head is plugged in to the same power pack, each head would receive 1000 watt-seconds, and the exposure for each head at the same distance would drop to f/16.

Figure B.3 The relationship between watt-second distribution from a power pack and exposure.

As you can see, together with distance, controlling the amount of energy from the power pack is another powerful way to control exposure in a lighting studio.

Another way to think of this is that controlling the amount of energy being distributed to each head is the dramatic way to control exposure in the studio, moving the lights back and forth is the subtle way to effect exposure. Even a small shift in the position of the lights makes a noticeable difference.

[note] Consult your power pack’s user’s manual for detailed instructions on how to achieve different ratios of exposure with that specific unit.

The Visual Qualities of Studio-lit Subjects

It is very easy to take for granted that any object visible in a photograph is simply being rendered as it is. But in fact, the shape and surface textures that we perceive in any photograph can be either enhanced or downplayed based on how it is lit.

Object Shape Depiction

Hard and Soft Shadows

The shape of an object is made visually clear by the way the attached and cast shadows are either flattened or enhanced based on the direction of the light falling on it.

  • Attached shadows are the shadows on the surface of the object.
  • Cast shadows are shadows created by light sources of the object that can appear on nearby surfaces.

Cast and attached shadows can be either hard or soft, depending on how abrupt or gradual the gradations or transitions are between areas of lightness and darkness.

The effective size of the light source determines the type of shadows produced in the following way:

  • Large light sources (relative to the subject) produce soft shadows.

    Figure B.4 Large light sources produce soft shadows.

  • Small light sources (relative to the subject) produce hard shadows.

    Figure B.5 Small light sources produce hard shadows.

Because the distance of the light source from the subject affects its apparent size:

  • Large/close light sources produce the softest light.
  • mall/distant light sources produce the hardest light.

We intuitively feel that the brightness of the light is the main factor affecting the hardness or softness of the shadows produced. This is not true. The “wrap-around” effect is the determining factor.

Often you need to fill the empty shadows of hard or softly lit subjects in lighting studio setups.

You can use a second strobe head, but you also can create an effective fill light by adding a reflective surface to the set.

Figure B.6 Hard light with a fill reflector.

Reflections and Surface Depiction

Surface reflections provide visual cues to our eyes on whether or not a surface is smooth and highly reflective, or rough and matte textured.

The following three types of reflections determine how a subject looks:

  • Diffuse reflections
  • Direct reflections
  • Glare reflections

  1. Diffuse reflections are produced by highly textured surfaces.

    Figure B.7 Diffuse reflections.

    • Because light scatters randomly from highly textured surfaces, the brightness of a diffuse reflection is not affected by the angle of the light source or the viewing angle.
    • The brightness of a diffuse reflection is affected only by the inverse square law (the distance between the light source and the surface).
    • White subjects primarily produce diffuse reflections.

  2. Direct reflections are produced by mirror-like surfaces.

    Figure B.8 Highly reflective surface with lights in and out of the reflection area.

    Direct, or “specular,” reflections bounce from mirror-like surfaces at the same angle that they strike them; therefore:

    • Any light source within the “Reflection Area” (between A and B) defined by the surface will be visible to the camera.
    • Any light source outside the reflection area (D) will be invisible to the camera.
    • The size of the highlight produced by a direct reflection will change as the light source gets bigger or smaller and as the distance between the light and the subject changes; the brightness of the highlight will not.
    • Black surfaces show direct reflections more than white surfaces.

    To illustrate this effect, Figure B.9 shows a piece of fine handmade jewelry lit by a light source that is positioned outside the pendant’s reflections area in part D of the previous figure.

    Figure B.9 Light positioned outside the pendant’s reflection area.

    Figure B.10 Light positioned inside the pendant’s reflection area (Pendant created by LaShaune Fitch).

    Figure B.10 shows what happens when the light source is positioned inside the pendant’s reflection area.

    Note that the background of these examples is the same brightness because it is a matte surface, so the light falling on it scatters in all directions and is therefore affected only by the inverse square law.

  3. Glare reflections behave like direct reflections except that they are polarized.
    • Glare reflections are half as bright as direct reflections.
    • Glare reflections can be controlled with polarizing filters.
    • Glossy, non-mirrored surfaces (polished wood or leather) produce polarized glare at an angle between 40 and 50 degrees.
    • Direct reflections can be turned into polarized glare by putting polarizing filters on the light and the camera lens.

Alternative Exposures: Mood Depiction

High and Low Key Lighting

The brightness of depicted objects has an effect on the subtle moods they generate in viewers. Normal exposures give the impression of subjects simply being well lit.

Figure B.11 Normal exposure.

High key lighting carries a feeling of lightness that is upbeat.

Figure B.12 High key exposure.

Low key lighting sets a mood that is more somber.

Figure B.13 Low key exposure.

To understand how to translate flash meter readings into alternative depictions of your image, remember the following principles:

  1. You must first determine what aperture your image requires for the necessary depth of field. Consider this to be the key aperture, and for this example, let’s say that it’s f/16.

    Figure B.14 Key aperture: f/16.

  2. If you adjust the light’s position and/or the settings of your power pack so that the meter recommends f/16, your image will be rendered to make a well-lit gray card appear as Zone V.

    Figure B.15 Normal exposure.

  3. If you adjust the light’s position and/or the settings of your power pack so that the meter recommends one stop less than this Zone V reading: f/11, but your lens is still set to the key aperture f/16, your image will be rendered as Zone IV and will appear low key (darker than normal).

    Figure B.16 Low key exposure.

  4. If you adjust the light’s position and/or the settings of your power pack so that the meter recommends one stop more than this Zone V reading, say f/22, but your lens is still set to the key aperture f/16, your image will be rendered as Zone IV and will appear high key (lighter than normal).

    Figure B.17 High key exposure.

If this procedure seems confusing, remember that the meter is always trying to render whatever amount of light it sees as Zone V.

This is why when it sees less light, it recommends a larger aperture (f/11 in this example); and when it sees more light, it recommends a smaller aperture (f/22 in this example).
