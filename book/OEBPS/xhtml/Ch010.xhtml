The Zone System and Digital Photography

Introduction

One interesting way to consider the profound transition fine art photographers have gone through in recent years is from the perspective of what it took to become a fully functional practitioner before the digital revolution began.

Not long ago, it was possible to be a completely self-contained photographic artist if you could afford the thousand dollars or so it took to purchase a decent camera, enlarger, timers, trays, and chemicals. Photographic paper was an ongoing expense, but with a little bit of rough carpentry, you could build a sink. And if the room could be made dark enough and if running water was available, you could produce work up to the limits of your talent and aspirations.

The first major shift came when masters such as William Eggleston, Ernst Hass, Susan Meiselas, Larry Burrows, and Alex Webb, among others, opened the doors of color photography for the rest of us. This was an important breakthrough, but one consequence of this was that it never became practical for photographers to maintain their own color labs. During this phase, almost everyone had his film processed at commercial labs, and it was fashionable to develop close friendships with the few independent master printers who operated color printing facilities. This loss of independence was simply the price you paid to be a working photographic artist.

For all the challenges of the digital revolution, one overarching benefit is that it is once again possible to create a fully professional working environment where you can work privately and independently for a relatively small investment. For some artists, the control and freedom this offers is a hugely important gain.

But, of course, there is a vast difference in ambience between a moody, dimly lit, and redolent darkroom and today’s photographic workstation with its monitors and inkjet printers. For many artists, this is a very sad loss. For others, it’s a dream come true.

And, of course, none of this would be worthwhile were it not for the fact that digital photography is capable of producing fine artwork of the highest quality. I first discovered this quite unexpectedly many years ago when I walked into a fine art photography gallery in San Francisco, and with a wry smile, the owner told me that he had some prints by Graham Nash that he thought I should see. To be honest, I wasn’t expecting them to be more than interesting backstage portraits of rock stars, but what happened next completely changed my sense of what a photographic print could be.

He took me to the print viewing room in the back of the gallery and from a drawer pulled a large print that at first didn’t seem unusual, that is, until I noticed that it had a deep and rich but somehow soft quality of blacks that I had never seen before. When I realized that it was printed on what looked to be thick fine art drawing paper and he told me it had been made with what he called an “Iris printer” attached to a computer, I was at first stunned and then amazed and delighted! I was having a completely photographic experience through a process I had never even heard of before.

In those days, Iris printers cost more than $100,000, so I knew that my photographic life wouldn’t change overnight, but suddenly there was a new horizon that seemed extremely interesting. Now, of course, for a number of very good reasons, digital photography has quickly become the dominant working method for all types of photographers. It is now possible to produce prints that are extraordinarily beautiful without exposing yourself to photochemistry; and the process is both incredibly flexible and fun, that is, if you enjoy working on images in front of a monitor.

The debate between “purists” and digital converts is the inevitable expression of personal aesthetic preferences more than a fundamental schism within photography itself. Ansel Adams was one of the first to recognize and adopt Polaroid materials, and I’m certain he would be using digital processes if they met his extremely high standards for print quality. As of this writing, artists, curators, collectors, conservators, and dealers have long agreed that the image quality and archival longevity of digital photographic prints qualify them as fine artworks.

But there are many, often contradictory, misconceptions about digital photography that we first have to discuss. On one hand, some people think that digital photography is a hopelessly complex process that only photo-geeks can master.

On the other hand, some of the obvious differences between the digital and traditional approaches give some photographers the impression that learning new exposure and contrast control techniques isn’t really necessary—as if digital processes could magically provide instant solutions to some of photography’s technical mysteries! Let’s take a look at some of these differences and see what they can teach us:

  • First, digital cameras give you a small, instant preview of your image, along with warnings about excessive contrast that may be beyond the range of the camera to render. This is like having a tiny, electronic preview which would seem to eliminate the need to precisely calculate your exposure in advance because you can always make adjustments as needed.
  • Also, with digital processes, there is no film to develop, and we learned in Chapter 6, “Development,” how important it is to be able to use film development times to control the contrast of your negatives to facilitate fine printing.
  • Finally, digital processes can move your images into the vast realm of Adobe Photoshop and the almost infinite number of ways it allows you to manipulate and enhance your prints.

But each of these factors contains insights into why it’s important to better understand and apply some of the basic principles of the Zone System—although using methods that are unique to digital photography.

For example, higher-end DSLR cameras like the Canon EOS series and the Nikon D300 offer a feature called Live View that simulates the exposure of digital images you are about to take. Live View enabled cameras use what essentially is a video-based image on your LCD Monitor to give you a way to preview your composition and exposure choices. I have found Live View to be a very useful feature, especially when used together with Zone System previsualization concepts and camera raw conversion software discussed later in this chapter.

The problem with relying on Live View for all of your digital exposure choices is that, as you will learn in the section below titled The Problem with Digital Shadows, there are very important technical reasons to intentionally slightly overexpose digital images, but Live View previews might encourage you to instead underexpose for purely aesthetic reasons. The Zone System provides you with a very precise and consistent method for accurately exposing for the highlights in ways that might not feel intuitive when looking at a Live View image on your camera’s LCD monitor.

One other very useful feature that some digital cameras offer is a histogram of the previewed image that can serve as a guide for adjusting your exposure. We will discuss how this is done later in this chapter.

Digital camera previews can also be deceptive because those small images are actually altered by the camera’s internal software based upon generalized assumptions about the way you will want the image to look. This automatic feature is often helpful, but sometimes it exaggerates problems that are actually easily correctable if you understand how to manipulate the contrast of digital image files.

[note] Many digital cameras offer the option of adjusting the settings for how the JPEG preview is displayed. Consult your user’s manual for information on how to do this.

Finally, although Photoshop is by far the most powerful and complex image-editing tool ever created for nonscientific purposes, there are practical limits to what you can do to correct digital images that are badly underexposed or have too much contrast and are overexposed. Understanding these limits is essential for getting the most out of your digital work. In this sense, film and digital photography have more in common than you might think at first.

This chapter has two related objectives. The first is to provide readers with a clear and simple method for obtaining the best possible quality in their digital photographs. The second goal is to provide a broad understanding of the basic technical concepts that underlie digital image processing. These two goals might seem to be the same, but they really aren’t.

If you are looking for a general understanding of digital exposure and contrast control so you can get better results, this text is structured so you can read straight through without getting bogged down in the mathematical details of issues such as bit depth and the digital linear effect. The Automatic Alternative

Many people new to digital photography quickly discover that they can obtain perfectly adequate results using the automatic metering systems provided by camera manufacturers to simplify the shooting process.

Automatic metering modes work well, but they necessarily compromise the quality of your work in situations in which a better understanding of photo technique would allow for more innovative results. If you look closely at most creative work, you will see that, very often, the beauty and meaning of the work depend on departures from “automatic” ways of shooting. The more you understand the formal and technical principles of digital photography, the more control you’ll have over the creative process.

Color Management

Unless you’re a very experienced digital image editor and printer, it’s probably a good idea for you to read Appendix A on the subject of color management before you attempt to apply what you will learn in this chapter. “Color management” is the term used to describe the process of calibrating, profiling, and setting up your system so that your printer produces images that match what you see on your monitor. Many photographers new to digital processes take this step for granted and end up frustrated and waste lots of ink and paper attempting to produce predictable results.

Scanning

As one of the two ways that photographic images enter the digital world (the other being shooting with a digital camera), scanning is an important topic of this book, especially as it relates to the issues of image resolution, bit depth, and image quality.

However, this chapter will not discuss the problems that result from scanning negatives or prints that are badly underexposed or overdeveloped. Reading the preceding chapters of this book will teach you how to avoid producing unworkable negatives and prints in the first place.

Digital and Film Photography

Similarities and Differences

When you’re working with professional digital SLR cameras, the basic photographic principles relating to apertures, depth of field, shutter speeds, metering, and so on are essentially the same as for film-based photography.

This means that photographers beginning to make the transition to digital imaging don’t have to relearn what they already know about basic photography, and that’s reassuring. But, as we will see, the fact that these basic photographic principles are being applied to digital media has real consequences when it comes to effective exposure and contrast control techniques.

To better understand how the two approaches compare, let’s begin with an overview of the digital and film-based photographic processes.

The Digital Photographic Process

Every photographic frame, digital or film, goes through four basic steps, and each has a dramatic effect on the quality of the final print:

  1. ISO Selection.
  2. Exposure.
  3. Processing.
  4. Printing.

Both film and digital photography share these steps, so it’s important to understand them. Experienced photographers take each of these steps very seriously and therefore get generally better results. Beginning or casual photographers don’t understand the implications of these steps and either neglect or try to automate them, which is why they generally get inconsistent or technically mediocre images in the end.

Using the film-based photographic process as a model, I would like to explain each step’s digital equivalent so you can understand why I’m recommending that you make certain decisions about how to expose and process your digital images. I realize that there may be readers of this edition who have never worked with film, and for them, the comparisons in this section will be a bit of history. As I move through these explanations, I’ll highlight a number of cardinal rules for digital photography that will give you better results.

[Step 1] ISO Selection

Before you do anything else when taking a picture, it’s important to know the sensitivity rating of the media you plan to use. With film photography, we’ve learned that we have to choose a faster film (higher ISO) if we’re planning to shoot under low-light conditions. The trade-off is that faster films produce grainier prints.

Digital photographic processes work in a similar way with two important differences:

  1. Digital cameras allow you to change the ISO for each frame you shoot, which gives you much greater flexibility. This can be an enormous advantage.
  2. Instead of film grain, using higher ISO speeds with digital cameras adds what’s called “noise,” or random pixels of either brightness and/or color in the shadow areas of your image.

Figure 10.1 Digital noise.

This means that digital underexposure has two consequences that you have to cope with instead of one: As with film, the underexposed shadow areas are too dark and lack detail, but with digital images, you also have to deal with noise in your shadows, which can be unsightly and distracting.

[note] The presence of digital noise illustrates one of the key differences between film and digital sensors. Changing the ISO on a digital camera amplifies the signal generated by the camera sensor, but this is not the same as making it more sensitive to light.

For these reasons, the first cardinal rule for digital photography is: Unless you want dark and noisy shadow areas for aesthetic reasons, with digital cameras always use the lowest ISO that makes sense in a given shooting situation. The deciding factor is usually the most practical shutter speed.

For many reasons ISO 100 has become the standard, but because digital technologies change rapidly, refer to your camera’s specifications to make sure. There are effective software tools you can use to minimize or totally eliminate noise from digital images, but using them can also degrade your image by obscuring fine detail if you don’t use them properly. (See Appendix X for a list of helper applications that I recommend.)

[Step 2] Exposure

Digital camera light meters behave in essentially the same way as those in film cameras: They both average the brightness of the reflected light they see to an exposure that prints as middle 18 percent gray or Zone V.*

You can test this very easily with a digital camera by shooting three frames one after the other in the following way. (Note that you can also perform this exercise using any camera phone, as illustrated in Figures 5.7–5.9 in Chapter 5, “Exposure.”)

  • Set your camera to one of its automatic metering modes, entirely fill the viewfinder with an evenly lit very dark surface, and shoot the first frame.
  • Using the same automatic metering mode, completely fill the second frame with a middle gray surface and shoot it.
  • Automatically meter and shoot the third frame filled with a very light surface.

When you look at the previews of these three frames, you will see that they’re very close in value and only the second frame actually looks like what you shot.

[note] To complete this little exercise you could shoot a frame that is half filled with light and dark surfaces. Both will be properly exposed. The broader implications of this effect are spelled out in Chapter 5.

Although digital and film camera meters behave in the same way, because of fundamental differences in the way that digital chips measure light, the basic rules for applying the Zone System to digital photography are the opposite of those for film.

The second cardinal rule for digital photography is: Expose for the highlights and process for the shadows.

This is just the opposite of the classic Zone System rule for film photography, where the rule is: Expose for the shadows and develop for the highlights. I fully explain why this is true in the later section “The Zone System of Digital Exposure.”

[Step 3] Processing: Camera Raw

After you expose a frame of normal photographic film, the image first exists in what’s called an invisible “latent” state. All the subject’s brightness values are captured and are encoded in the emulsion, but they aren’t visible until after the film is “developed” in the three baths called Developer, Stop, and Fixer. After development, the visible image is fixed in the negative and there isn’t much you can do to change it.

I know that all this information is familiar and perfectly obvious to most readers of this book, but I’m overstating it to make the point that, conceptually, a very similar process applies to digital photography, and this has profound implications for how you should work with digital images.

After you expose the digital camera sensor to light from the subject, the image first exists in a latent state known as “Digital Raw,” which contains visual information that can’t be seen until the image has been processed or “converted” into a form that can be viewed on a digital monitor, either as the small preview on your camera, or as the image you see on your computer screen.

The development or conversion process is done electronically, and if you don’t preserve this raw image, all the information it contains is lost forever. This would be like throwing away your negative after making your first print.

So, another fundamental difference between digital processes and film is that the unprocessed, digital latent or raw state of the image can be preserved even after the image has been processed (converted). Latent film images are always lost when the film is developed.

Just as with film photography, you can easily tell the difference between unconverted and converted digital images: On your computer screen, you see raw images as generic icons with extensions like .nef, .crw, or .dng, which stands for the universal standard “Adobe Digital Negative.” The reason the raw image icon is generic is that, unless your operating system has a built-in raw converter, your computer has no idea of what the unconverted image looks like; just as with film if it were possible to see an undeveloped frame with visible light.

The developed or converted form of the image has a thumbnail image icon and an extension like the familiar .jpg, .jpgf, or .psd for images that have been converted into Photoshop files.

Photographers new to the digital world are often confused about this step because the conversion actually happens twice and in different ways in the digital process. The first time the image is converted is in a generalized way by JPEG compression into the thumbnail you see on the camera’s small screen. This preview is based on default settings built into the camera and is only a rough approximation of the second, more refined conversion you can do after the image has been downloaded to your computer.

But the point remains, digital images can exist in two distinct states—raw and converted— just like undeveloped and processed film. The differences between these two states is extremely important to understand because raw digital image files contain vastly more visual information than the converted state, especially when the conversion is into the popular JPEG format used by many photographers and applications.

JPEG-formatted images are compressed and processed into a form that is typically two to four times smaller than the raw file of the same image, so it can never make a print that is as large with the same quality.

The raw digital image not only has much more visual information, but also has information called “metadata” that records things like the exposure you used, the focal length of the lens, and the ISO used. Some cameras are even equipped with GPS software that allows you to know precisely where and when the image was taken!

[note] JPEG images also have metadata, but because the image is so compressed, this is much less useful.

These days the advantages of shooting in raw format are becoming common knowledge for photographers and there are even smartphones that can capture raw images. The iPhone 7 Plus is a recent and very good example of a smartphone that captures in a raw format* and, with its 12 megapixel sensor, generates image files that produce large and beautiful prints. But there are still many new digital photographers who either inadvertently buy cameras that don’t offer the raw format as an option, or they convert their images to JPEGs and throw this information away without realizing what they are doing. If nothing else, you should make a practice of archiving the raw version of your best work, just as you would your negatives.

The vital point to know is that, if you choose to shoot your digital images in JPEG format, the conversion and compression happens immediately, and this information-rich raw latent image is instantly erased.

This gives us our third cardinal rule for digital photography: Always shoot in raw format unless you are certain that you will never need the visual information stored in the raw digital file.

It’s true that shooting in the raw format gives you fewer images per storage card or disk, but because you will never have to buy film for your camera, it’s economical to buy a larger storage media and convert your images to JPEG format if you need to later.

[Step 4] Printing

There are profound differences between the digital and analog photographic printing methods. Digital inkjet printer manufacturers strive to make the prints from their products match the quality of fine color and black-and-white silver-based prints, but there will always be differences that are obvious to experts. Whether one is qualitatively better than the other is something that will always be debated, but this clearly has more to do with the aesthetic skills and vision of the maker than anything else.

There are also some important formal process issues that, for many of us, make digital photographic printing an exciting advance over traditional printing.

When you create an analog print in the darkroom, part of the mystique and challenge is that you have to train your body to precisely reproduce the pattern of hand movements required to burn and dodge the print properly. If you are able to do this once, perfect; it is very difficult to do that again. This has the result—for some, the benefit—of making each analog print unique. However, fine printers often feel that there is only one special print that is absolutely perfect. Often at this point the artist is exhausted and has to struggle to retrieve the precise choreography of hand motions that are required to return to that magical stage in the process.

When you are working on fine printing a digital image in Adobe Photoshop, you still have to work hard to achieve this ideal rendition, but when you’re done, the image stays at that level of resolve until you are ready to print it or push it further. This, in many ways, is a degree of control and refinement that serious photo-artists have always wanted. But, of course, all this assumes that three crucial elements in the process are in place:

  • First, all of the technical components of your system (software, monitor, printer, etc.) have to be well calibrated and fully functional.
  • Second, you need to have a clear understanding of the relevant concepts and processes involved.
  • And finally, you need the aesthetic judgment and vision to place all this into effective motion. The purpose of this chapter is to help provide the first two of these factors.

Although it’s true that in purely formal terms you can produce effects with digital printing processes that would be difficult or impossible to reproduce in a darkroom, I firmly believe that it’s important for photographers to internalize the skills that come from using your hands and body in a traditional darkroom before devoting your time to digital printing. But then again, I know that this is a bias that’s tinged with nostalgia. It will be interesting to see how photographers who are just beginning respond to developing their aesthetics in a purely digital environment. My guess is that digital photography will simply be another tool that expands rather than radically reforms the way artists use it to express themselves.

Other Digital Printing Considerations: Large-Scale and Banner Printing

One of the key advantages of digital photographic printing is the option it offers to create prints that are far larger than could be printed in the average traditional darkroom. One of the great joys of visiting Ansel Adams’s home darkroom in Carmel was to see the massive horizontal enlarger he used to make large-scale fine black-and-white prints. Black-and-white mural printing requires great skill and special darkroom layouts. Color mural printing requires a profitable business to support the equipment and chemistry needed. In contrast, professional-quality inkjet printers that make beautiful black-and-white or color images up to 44 inches wide are available as of this writing for under $5,000.

In most major cities, it’s possible to find commercial digital printing labs capable of printing digital files up to 72 inches or larger. The key to printing this way successfully is knowing how to properly prepare your file through interpolation in Adobe Photoshop using the Bicubic Smoother (best for enlargement) setting inside the Image Size command box and understanding the quality limits of digital printing. I’ll have more to say about the Image Size box in the later section on digital scanning.

The other advantage of digital printing is the ability to create artworks in banner or scroll format with prints as long as you need them to be. I, for example, have prints in my portfolio that are over 6 feet long. Printing these using traditional methods would be prohibitively expensive.

Pixels: Size, Resolution, Bit Depth, and Quality

The representational power of digital photography begins with individual picture elements called “pixels.” If you enlarge any digital image enough, you will see that the apparently continuous tonalities of the image are actually created from these tiny individual image tiles.

And yet, despite how familiar we are with the general idea of pixels, there is still a lot of confusion about what they actually are.

There are four essential points you need to understand about pixels, and each has important implications for the quality of digital photographs:

  1. There are two general types of pixels: fixed-sized hardware pixels and variable-sized image pixels.
  2. When there are 200 or more pixels per inch in an image, your unaided eyes can’t see them.
  3. Although image pixels can be almost any size, from fractions of an inch to many feet across, in black-and-white digital images each pixel can be only one individual tone of black, gray, or white. In color digital images, each pixel can be only one single color.
  4. Although each individual pixel in an image can be only one tone or color, the number of different tones or colors a pixel is allowed to be can be vast!

Figure 10.2 Digital image pixels.

To visualize what I mean by this fourth point, imagine that two children are asked to fill in an empty square outline with one color from a box of crayons. Because this square is equivalent to a pixel, each child can use only one color to fill it in.

The difference I’m referring to is equivalent to one of these children having a box with only 10 crayons in it, whereas the other has 500. Clearly, the second child has many more and subtle colors to choose from and a great aesthetic advantage. This difference in the range of available color choices is referred to in digital photography as differences in “bit depth.”

Hardware Pixels and Image Pixels

Hardware pixels are physical things built into digital sensor chips, computer monitors, scanners, and inkjet printers.

The function of hardware pixels is to gather, display, and reproduce the visual information that we see as image pixels.

  • For example, in a digital camera, the image sensor that takes the place of the film is covered with hardware pixels called “photosites” that actually capture the image that passes through the camera lens.
  • On the surface of a computer monitor, a “pixel” is a fixed and rigid physical entity that can never change size. The resolution of monitors is set to values like 96 or 110 pixels per inch. Retina Displays have 200 to 326 pixels per inch. Whatever the resolution of display monitors, this pixel count always remains the same. But keep in mind that these “monitor pixels” are only the tiny dots that actually create the “image pixels” you see on the screen.

When the screen is displaying a digital image, these “screen pixels” are used to create “image pixels” that can be almost any size depending on how many screen pixels comprise them. For example, when one screen pixel is used to reproduce one image pixel, the image display in Photoshop reads 100 percent, or “Actual size.” When two screen pixels are used to display one image pixel, the image shrinks to 50 percent.

Another example of this relationship between fixed and variable image pixels would be the physical holes in the print heads of inkjet printers that spread minute clouds of pigment on paper to reproduce the image pixels we see in prints. (See Appendix C for more details about pixel size relationships.)

Resolution and Bit Depth

When a digital photograph is either displayed or printed, the number of pixels per inch that you see is defined as the image’s “resolution.” This is one key factor that determines a digital image’s quality. For example, if there are 300 pixels in one inch of an image, we would consider that to be a high-quality image. Recall that in the “Pixels: Size, Resolution, Bit Depth, and Quality” section we stated in point #2 that 200 pixels per inch are enough to fool your eye that there are no pixels present at all. If there are only 10, we would say the image quality was very poor. Much more on this topic shortly.

The number of different tones or colors that a pixel is allowed to be in a digital image is a measure of what’s known as the pixel’s “bit depth.” This is another key determinant of a digital image’s quality.

As you will see from the following examples, learning how to optimize both the resolution and the bit depth of an image’s pixels are important skills for digital photographers who want the most from their work.

The Quality of Digital Images

It’s always a little ironic when someone looks at a fine digital print and says that it looks just like a “real” photograph. In all the ways that matter the most, even a portrait of your child in your iPhone is a real photograph.

Using these two gradations as examples, we can now say that what digital photographers mean when they speak of “image quality” is the goal of reproducing smooth, gradually continuous gradations that are typical of analog imaging media like film.

For reasons that will soon be clear, Figure 10.6 has noticeable steps in the transitions from one tone to the next. The more smooth and seamless the transitions are from one tonal value to the next, the higher the image quality is said to be.

Given what we learned earlier about the way pixels seem to disappear when there are more than 200 of them per inch, you might think that only small pixels (high resolution) are enough to achieve a smooth gradation. The following example will illustrate why both resolution and bit depth are needed to determine the quality or smoothness of the gradations shown in the preceding figures.

Imagine that each pixel in the preceding gradations is a step in a flight of stairs. The “resolution” of the staircase would be a measure of the number of steps in each staircase. The actual size of each individual step (pixel) is the factor; the smaller the step is, the more of them there are and the more smooth the gradation.

But keep in mind that although the size of each pixel (step) can change, it can still be only one tonal value. In this case, each pixel is limited to one of only 10 different tonal values.

Notice that by greatly increasing the resolution of Figure 10.7B, its slope is much smoother. It has much smaller pixels (steps), but the gradation itself is still not continuous because the number of individual tonal values that each step is allowed to be in this illustration is still limited to 10. This is like the child who had only 10 crayons in her box in the earlier example.

Remember that we defined bit depth as the number of different tonal values (also known as “levels”) that each pixel is allowed to be in a given image. With this definition in mind, we could say that Figure 10.7B is an example of a high-resolution but low bit depth image.

Increasing an image’s bit depth allows each pixel to be more levels of tonality. If we were to optimize both the resolution and the bit depth of this illustration, the result would look like Figure 10.8.

The reason this gradation is smooth is that the number of steps is very high (in other words, it has high resolution), and the number of different tones each step is allowed to be is also high (high bit depth). What this illustration makes clear is that learning how to optimize both a digital image’s resolution and its bit depth is important for achieving the best quality in your work.

Optimizing Digital Image Resolution

Pixel editing software makes it possible to radically alter many characteristics of digital images, which include the size and color of pixels and your image’s contrast and sharpness, along with many other variables.

In this sense, bit depth and resolution can be considered core digital values because the only time it’s possible to establish these qualities is when the image is captured, by either a digital camera or a scanner. The reason is that bit depth and resolution are functions of the amount of memory your scanner or digital camera commits to actually gathering visual information from your subject. To use the crayon analogy one more time, you need a larger box (more memory) to hold a larger number of crayons (bit depth and resolution). The point here is that when the subject is out of sight, it is no longer possible to change either the core resolution or bit depth of your image.

[note] The other technical factors that influence the quality of a digital image include the quality of the sensor and the analog-to-digital converter built into the scanner or camera.

Digital editing software provides you with very tempting options for increasing the virtual (as opposed to the actual) resolution and bit depth of digital images if you discover that the resolution is too low after your image has already been scanned or captured. This is called “interpolation.”

The problem is that when you interpolate, you are asking the computer to mathematically simulate visual information that it can’t actually see. Over recent years the algorithms used by digital editing software have dramatically improved, but if interpolation is pushed too far, this will result in a noticeable loss of image quality.*

Understanding how to optimize a digital image’s resolution begins with having a very clear idea of how you ultimately intend to display your photograph. The ideal resolution for an image that will be displayed on the web is very different from one that you intend to print with an inkjet printer. The more thought you give to this decision at the beginning of the process, the more you are likely to avoid painful choices in the end.

For this reason, our next cardinal rule is: Capture or scan at resolutions and bit depths based on the type and ultimate size of your final image and don’t interpolate unless it can’t be avoided.

There are only two ways of bringing photographic information into the digital realm: digital capture or digital scanning. Digital capture means that you are using a digital camera to directly translate the visual world into digital information. Digital scanning assumes that you already have a film negative, slide, or print that you want to manipulate with digital processes. In both cases, you need to understand how to optimize your bit depth and resolution.

Optimizing Bit Depth through Digital Capture

Earlier when discussing the difference between shooting in Camera Raw format and JPEG, I emphasized the fact that raw format images contain vastly more visual information than compressed JPEGs.

Now that I’ve defined some of these terms, I can further say that when you shoot in Camera Raw format, your images will automatically have the high bit depth you are looking for to optimize the quality of your digital photographs. And, in fact, this is the only way that you can obtain high bit depth images through digital capture.

In Appendix D, I discuss some of the arithmetic behind the terms “high” and “low” bit depth. This underscores why it is so important to shoot your digital images as Camera Raw formatted files before you then convert them into any of the processed formats that you require: .psd, .jpgf, or .jpg.

Optimizing Resolution through Digital Capture: Pixel Count and Print Size

When you remember once again that it takes only a minimum of 200 pixels per inch to fool your eye into believing that it’s seeing a continuous toned gradation, it becomes easier to understand why digital photographers are concerned with the resolution or pixel count of the cameras they buy. Here’s how this works.

If your goal is to make the largest possible, high-quality (meaning photorealistic) digital print, the rule is: The greater the number of pixels your camera can capture, the larger the print you can make.

The “resolution” of a digital camera’s image sensor is defined as the actual count of the individual “photosites” (pixels) on the surface of the sensor. When a camera lists its resolution as 10 megapixels, this means that if you multiply the number of tiny photosites in the horizontal and vertical dimensions, you get the total number of photosites on the sensor.

For example, imagining that Figure 10.9 is the image sensor of a typical digital SLR, if the manufacturer designs the sensor so that there are 3,872 photosites in the horizontal dimension and 2,592 in the vertical, the total number of photosites would equal 10,036,224 pixels. This would be called a “10-megapixel sensor.”

Figure 10.9 The dimensions of a 10-megapixel digital image sensor.

So the question is: How large a print can you make if you have approximately 10 million pixels to work with from your digital camera?

Here are the factors to consider. If your goal is to create an image that is photorealistic and you’re using a high-quality inkjet printer, then the standard number of pixels you need would be 300 pixels per inch. In this case, 200 pixels per inch are enough to render what seems to be a continuous gradation, and the extra 100 are added for good measure.

Starting with the image sensor’s long dimension of 3,872 pixels, if you divide this number of pixels by 300 pixels per inch, the result is a print that is about 13″ × 8.6″, keeping the proportions of the image the same.

Figure 10.10 Pixel count and final print size at 300 pixels per inch.

You can now understand why professional photographers often work with digital camera backs that capture up to 80 megapixels. With that many pixels, you could make a large inkjet print without interpolation.

So, the key to optimizing resolution in digital capture is to understand the size of the prints you intend to create and use the formulas just described to purchase a camera with the appropriate resolution.

Optimizing Resolution and Bit Depth through Digital Scanning

Digital scanning assumes that you already have a film negative or slide to work with, which means that eventually scanning could be a highly specialized or lost art. But for now, using a virtual drum scanner, like the Hasselblad/Imacon Flextight scanner described below, to produce high-resolution scans from fine-grained color films remains a key way to create extremely high-quality digital image files. For example, if you were to use an Imacon scanner to scan finegrained medium to large format film, you could end up with the equivalent of a 200 megapixel digital image. This is more resolution than you can currently get from a Phase One digital back costing well over $30,000.

Also, learning how to optimize a digital scan presents you with an ideal opportunity to learn how to control the core values of resolution and bit depth. Scanner interfaces present you with opportunities to actually decide on settings that determine the resolution and bit depth of the resulting digital image. By looking closely and working with digital images from scans, you can see right away the consequences of making wrong choices.

Scanner types fall into categories defined by the material they are designed to digitize and the level of quality they can produce. It is also vital to understand the difference between the actual optical resolution capabilities of a scanner—in other words, the amount of information it can literally see, as opposed to the virtual or advertised resolution of the scanner, that is, the amount of information it can fake through interpolation. Read the scanner’s specifications carefully and always use a scanner’s optical resolution as your standard. Avoid interpolation unless you have no other choice.

[note] Adobe Photoshop’s interpolation technology has improved to the point where you can do minor upsampling without noticeably degrading your image. However, if you decide that major increases of your image’s file size are necessary, consider using special applications designed for this purpose. (See Appendix X for Photoshop-related applications.)

Dedicated Film Scanners

Dedicated film scanners are designed to produce high-quality scans of 35mm film. You should always scan at no less than 4,000 dots per inch (dpi) and set the scale size to 100 percent. Anything more than these settings is interpolation. This process will result in an image that has 4,000 dpi resolution but is tiny: 1″ × 1.5″ (this is 100 percent of the size of a standard 35mm frame). But remember that you can always redistribute the scanned pixels to end up with a larger image using Photoshop’s Image Size command. The trick is to uncheck the Resample Image box as illustrated later.

Medium and Large Format Scanners

There are three types of larger format scanners: professional drum, Hasselblad/Imacon Flextight, and flatbed scanners. Drum and Flextight scanners always produce higher-quality scans, but they aren’t always essential for digital photographers working with current inkjet printers.

Earlier, we discussed the direct relationship between the resolution of your digital file and the size of the high-quality print you can produce. With this is mind, you can easily understand the key advantage of drum scanners. Scanners of this type are capable of producing digital image files of up to 11,000 dpi, but this is often much more resolution than the average digital photographer will need. The two disadvantages of using drum scanners are that they cost from $16,000 to $65,000, and they usually require that the negative or slide be wet mounted to the drum. If this procedure is not done carefully, there is the possibility of damaging your film.

Hasselblad/Imacon Flextight scanners are relatively less expensive, but because they cost around $12,000, most photographers will rely on the services of commercial digital labs to produce scans of their work.

Professional-quality flatbed scanners are currently priced well within the means of most photographers, and they have the advantage of being able to scan film negatives, positives, and prints and documents. For this reason, the next section of this chapter will outline the steps for producing optimal digital scans using software and scanners that are standards in this field: Adobe Photoshop CC 2015.5 and an Epson Perfection V700 Photo Scanner.

Guidelines For Fine Digital Printing

Pixel resolution, bit depth, and the image’s total file size are core digital values that together determine the quality of the final print you can make from a given digital image file.

The following guidelines will ensure that the quality of your work is as good as it can possibly be.

  1. Always carefully consider the ultimate goal of your project.
    • If your intention is to make a fine inkjet print, the key factor to consider is the size of the largest print you plan to make.

  2. Always capture your images at the highest bit depth possible to facilitate image editing.
    • For digital cameras, this means shooting in your camera’s raw format.
    • For scanned images, this means choosing the highest bit depth allowed by your scanner.

  3. Always scan your image at the resolution that is optimal for the final result you are intending.
    • For inkjet prints, this should be 240, 300, or 360 dpi.
    • For offset printing, this is 300 dpi.
    • For web-based images, 72 dpi is sufficient.

  4. After you have edited your high bit depth image, you can reduce it to 8 bits per channel before sending it to your printer, although, as noted previously, 16-bit workflows are now possible.

Learning how to appropriately maximize the resolution and bit depth of your images is important, but we now need to consider the role that proper exposure plays in this process.

Bit Depth and Digital Exposure

It’s often hard to believe that beautiful digital prints are ultimately created from numbers that could just as easily be printed out in undecipherable rows and columns. Nothing demonstrates this fact more clearly than the issues of bit depth and exposure with digital cameras.

Earlier in this book, we learned how the Zone Scale functions as a bridge between the meter numbers we use to measure subject values and contrast, and the tonal values of fine photographic prints. In digital photography, the same role is played by something called the histogram.

Understanding Histograms

Recall that bit depth is a measure of the number of different tonal values that a pixel is allowed to be in a digital image file. The more distinct tonal levels there are in an image, the more photorealistic it will look. But it has been demonstrated experimentally that using only 200 individual tones is enough for human beings to believe that they are looking at a continuous-toned reproduction. This is like saying that if a mural artist were given a massive pile of little tiles and that each had one of 200 different tones, she could create an image that looked like Figure 10.15.

Figure 10.15 Digital image with adequate bit depth.

If, however, she were given tiles that had only 100 different tonal values, she would have to make a mural that looked like Figure 10.16.

Figure 10.16 Digital image with low bit depth.

The harshness and lack of subtlety in Figure 10.16 is the result of the limited number of pixel tonal levels in the image.

This example leads directly to the question of how bit depth is accounted for in digital photography. For most digital editing purposes, the minimum standard for quality is what is called an “8-bit” image, where each pixel can be any one of 256 tones from black to white. (See Appendix D for an explanation of what “8 bit” means numerically.)

In Adobe Photoshop histograms, these individual pixel tonal levels are numbered from 0 for the blackest possible pixel to 255 for pure white pixels. This, again, is like having a choice of 256 values of tiles to choose from when you are building a mosaic mural.

The pixel levels in digital images are organized into layers that are called “channels.” An 8-bit black-and-white image has one channel called “Gray” with pixels that can be any of 256 different levels of tone.

Figure 10.17 8 Bit Gray channel with 256 pixel tonal levels.

Color digital images have three channels—Red, Green, and Blue—that are combined to create mixed colors. In 8-bit color images, each of these channels also has pixels that can be 256 levels of the red, green, and blue colors. This adds up to about 16.7 million different color possibilities (256 × 256 × 256 = 16,777,216).

With 16-bit RGB color images, when you multiply 65,536 tones per channel times three channels (red/green/blue), you end up with a total of over 281 trillion possible tones for each pixel (65,536 × 65,536 × 65,536 = 281474976710656)!

Figure 10.18 16 Bit Color channels with 65,536 pixel tonal levels each.

To understand how histograms come into being from all of this, imagine how it would look if we took a black-and-white image and selected 256 of its differently toned tiles (pixels) and laid them down in a line with the darkest one on the left and the lightest one on the right.

For Figure 10.19, I drew this line of tiny pixels above the familiar 10-step Zone Scale, but remember that there are actually 256 sections, or “Tonal Levels,” in the histogram line.

If someone were to ask you which pixel in this line is exactly the same tonal value as a specific tone of gray in a digital photograph, you could measure that spot with a device designed for that purpose, and it would give you the exact pixel reading you were looking for. If the print value in question happened to be middle gray, its pixel value number would be 128, or halfway between 0 and 255.

Figure 10.19 256 pixel tonal levels and the Zone Scale.

If you then wanted to illustrate how many pixels with the value of 128 there were in that image, you could either stack them up or draw a tall line if there were a lot of pixels with that tonal value or a shorter line if there were only a few.

If a given pointer is below an empty space in the histogram, that means no pixels in the image have these values.

Stretching the Histogram: Defining the Problem of Digital Exposure

So far what we have is a static representation of the pixels in this digitized image. To understand how digital editing software tools use histograms to manipulate the contrast of digital photographs and how all this relates to digital exposure, begin by imagining that you are trying to reproduce a smooth, continuous gradation by drawing very thin lines of tonal values on a strip of rubber. (If you think of each line as a pixel tonal level, this becomes a very good analogy.)

If you use enough lines of tone and apply them very carefully, it’s possible to create a convincing illusion of seamlessness.

Figure 10.22 Digital gradation on a rubber strip.

If you then wanted to increase the representational contrast of this gradation by making the lighter end white so that the two edges of this gradation were further apart, you could think of this as “increasing its dynamic range.” The simplest way to do this would be to stretch out the rubber strip as far as you wanted it to go.

Figure 10.23 Rubber digital gradation with its contrast increased.

The contrast of this gradation is now greater. But notice that, because the rubber gradient has been stretched, this has the effect of separating the individual lines of the gradation and breaking up the illusion of continuity. The inherently digital nature of the representation is suddenly revealed.

Expanding and compressing the contrast of digital images is one of the basic functions of pixel-editing software, and when these gaps appear between pixel tonal levels in a digital photograph, it’s called “banding” or posterization.

Figure 10.24 Digital banding.

Learning how to minimize this effect is an important skill for digital photographers who are trying to optimize the quality of their work.

Photographic film has no problem reproducing continuous gradations and increasing and compressing their contrast because they are seamless by nature. (Film grain isn’t a factor in this discussion because even a grainy gradation still progresses from one tone to the next in a continuous but grainy way.)

When we use this illustration as a guide, the two fundamental principles that help to minimize banding makes intuitive sense:

  1. First, consider that the more lines you are able to draw on the rubber strip, the more even and continuous the result will be and the more stretching it can tolerate before banding appears. In other words, higher bit depth images are much easier to edit without compromising their quality.
  2. The less you stretch the rubber strip, the more the illusion of a continuous gradation is preserved.

I mentioned earlier that black-and-white JPEG 8-bit digital images allow for pixels to be any of 256 different tones from black to white. But Camera Raw photographs can easily be converted into 16-bit images where each pixel can be any one of 65,536 levels of tone or color! This means that you will have 256 times the number of pixel levels to work with, and this makes avoiding banding much easier. (See Appendix D for more on bit depth.)

The second principle has direct implications for how digital images should be exposed. Here’s how this works. Our rubber gradation illustration makes it clear that the more you stretch out the tonal values of your subject, the more the image is degraded into noticeable digital bands. The following illustration demonstrates the relationship this principle has to exposure in digital cameras. The Zone System of Digital Exposure

The second is more subtle but just as important and is discussed in the next section, “The Problem of Digital Shadows.”

The Problem of Digital Shadows

The reason this process is especially damaging to the shadow areas is because of the second, more subtle reason I mentioned earlier. The fact is that current digital cameras inherently devote more levels of brightness values to rendering detail in the highlights than they do to the darker areas of the subject.

The further they are away from the darker end of the histogram, the better. The Zone System provides you with a simple way to achieve this result.

Digital Exposing for the Highlights

It should now be clear that there are important technical advantages to properly exposing digital image files, but using the Zone System with 35mm cameras always requires some extra effort.

With roll-film cameras, the problem always is how to apply individual contrast control to frames that must be developed together. That isn’t a problem with digital 35mm frames, but there is still the issue of metering selected areas with built-in light meters.

Because it’s so easy to preview digital photographs using the camera’s LCD window, one practical solution would be to take a quick reference meter reading of your subject and note where the histogram falls on the scale from right to left.

Figure 10.35 Camera histogram of underexposed digital image.

If the image is too dark, you could simply open up one or two stops using either apertures or shutter speeds until the histogram is as far right as it needs to be to avoid the problems caused by expanding the contrast.

Moving the histogram levels too far to the right would be overexposure and cause the subject’s highlight values to fall off the edge of the histogram, where they would be lost. (The consequences of this will be discussed in the section “The Zone System and Digital Contrast Control.”) This is a quick and very simple exposure method (and this is what many digital photographers actually do), but there is one issue that, if you’re shooting raw image files, makes this approach less precise than it appears to be.

Figure 10.36 Camera histogram of digital image exposed to the right.

As I mentioned at the beginning of this chapter, digital camera previews are generated based on assumptions about how you will eventually want to use the image. One of those assumptions is that you’re shooting for JPEG images that are compressed. If you are shooting in raw format, which you should be, this means that the histogram the camera creates won’t precisely match the one you will eventually be working with. This issue also applies to the flashing “out of gamut” highlight warnings that are a function of many digital SLR cameras. When your preview is set to this function, it can give you the alarming impression that your highlights are blown out when, if you are shooting in raw format, they may in fact be recoverable.

As mentioned at the beginning of this chapter, higher-end DSLRs offer Live View functions that are very useful for previsualization. But, a more precise exposure approach would be one that accurately placed your subject’s highlight values where they should be on the Zone Scale. Photographers who are experienced with properly exposing transparency films are very familiar with this variation of the Zone System, which is much more accurate and consistent.

If your in-camera light meter has a spot metering function or if you have a handheld spot meter, follow these steps:

  1. Carefully meter the Zone VII Important Highlights of your subject.
  2. Make note of the meter’s recommended exposure for these areas.
  3. Because Zone V is two stops darker than Zone VII, opening up two stops from the meter’s recommended exposure for the textured highlight will accurately place them on Zone VII.

Figure 10.37 Meter’s recommended exposure: Zone VII on Zone V. Remember that the meter’s recommended exposure would render these areas as Zone V or middle gray.

Figure 10.38 Important Highlight placed on Zone VII.

This will be the perfect exposure for digital cameras! Chapters 4, “The Zone,” and 5, “Exposure,” explain the general theory behind this process in great detail.

With compressed 8-bit JPEG photographs, you have relatively few pixel levels to work with, so the editing required to correct bad exposures can seriously degrade your image. Although this Zone System-oriented approach to digital exposure will work extremely well with JPEG images, it’s important not to overstate the importance of using this method when you’re shooting Camera Raw files for your general purposes.

As you will see, the conversion process from raw files to tone-mapped digital images offers you many powerful tools for manipulating the exposure and contrast of your photographs. Adobe’s Camera Raw converter allows for effective exposure control that can, to some extent, compensate for exposure and contrast problems. What the Zone System adds to this process is the ability to make consistently precise exposures that require less correction in the conversion stage; it allows you to think creatively about how to use exposure in imaginative ways.

A Summary of Digital Exposure Effects

Let’s summarize what we have learned about digital exposure:

  1. It’s important to shoot using the lowest ISO that is practical in a given lighting situation. This will minimize the noise in your images. The higher the ISO, the more noise there will be in the shadow areas of your images.
  2. You should always shoot in Camera Raw format unless you’re sure you will never need the extra bit depth that this setting provides. This will give you many more tonal levels to work with when you’re editing your photographs.
  3. Underexposure of digital images results in a histogram that has its brightness levels clustered to the left where there are inherently fewer levels defining the shadows of your subject.
  4. This requires you to stretch out the histogram to enhance the image’s contrast, which causes banding that has its greatest effect on the shadow values of your subject.
  5. The solution is to expose for the highlights, which reduces the need to expand the image’s contrast and minimizes banding in the shadow areas.
  6. Properly applied, this method will leave you with digital files that are ideally suited for the contrast control methods, which are described in the next section.

The Zone System and Digital Contrast Control

In Chapter 2, “Print Quality, Negative Contrast, and Dynamic Range,” I used the myth of the ancient diabolical innkeeper Procrustes as an analogy for how the Zone System allows you to modify the contrast of your negatives so that they print well on the grade of photographic paper that you prefer for your work.

The myth tells the story of how Procrustes would either stretch his unwitting shorter clients on a rack until they were long enough for his bed or chop off their legs if they were too tall. This is similar to the way that the Zone System allows you to systematically either expand or compress the contrast of your negatives so that they match the “Procrustean Bed” of your chosen photographic paper grade.

Here’s a quick review of how this process works. The first step is to carefully meter the subject area that you want to be Zone III, dark with full texture and detail.

We have learned that light meters always recommend exposures for what they see that will print as Zone V or middle gray. Therefore, if we were to use the meter’s recommended exposure for this area, the negative would be overexposed by two stops.

Figure 10.39 Zone V placement of an area previsualized as Zone III.

Stopping down two stops will place the meter reading for the area previsualized as Zone III on Zone III where it belongs. If, as in this example, the Important Highlight falls on the appropriate Zone, then the negative would receive Normal Development.

Figure 10.40 Zone III placement of an area previsualized as Zone III.

If the Important Highlight reading falls too low on the Zone Scale, we would need to increase the development time to stretch the contrast of the negative until it matches the paper’s tonal scale. This is called N + Development; in this case, N + 2.

Figure 10.41 The effect of Normal Plus Two Development.

If the subject’s Important Highlights fall too high on the scale, we reduce the negative development time enough to compress the highlights until they fit properly on the scale. This is known as N– Development; in this case, N – 2.

Figure 10.42 The effect of Normal – 2 Development.

The reason this process works is that film is an inherently analog, continuous-tone medium that, within limits, allows for the compression or expansion of negative contrast without the problems we discussed that are associated with digital processes.

Many photographers have had the experience of seeing a negative that has detail in highlight areas that’s visible when viewed against a strong enough light source but simply won’t print on contemporary photographic papers. This demonstrates that the detail was present in the latent state of that image and could have been rendered printable if the development time had been correct. Digital imaging processes behave in a very different way.

The Limits of Digital Photography

Digital and film photography share one common goal: To capture as much subject information as possible so that it’s available to work with when you make your finished print.

In film, severe underexposure results in shadow detail that’s lost in clear, empty areas of film. Extreme overdevelopment creates highlight film densities that are opaque and print as empty pure white areas. Similar problems exist in the world of digital photography, but the causes and terms we use to describe them are different.

Underexposed digital images aren’t only dark and lack shadow detail; they also have dark tonal values that are contaminated with “noise” or random patterns of bright and colored pixels that degrade the quality of the image. This is especially true when you shoot digital photographs using higher ISO ratings like 1600 to 3200. This means that with digital materials you have to be concerned both with your shadow areas being too dark and lacking detail and with noise as an aesthetic factor.

[note] As of this writing there have been major advances in the digital image sensors of mid- to high-end professional cameras so that it is now possible to shoot at ISOs equivalent to 51200 or higher.

Figure 10.43 Digital noise in a high-contrast image.

For this illustration, I made the shadow area lighter to make the noise more visible.

At the other end of the scale, traditionally film has generally been more forgiving when it comes to recording high-contrast subjects. The ability of an imaging medium to record subject contrast is called its dynamic range. Dynamic range is usually measured in the number of f/stops you can increase your exposure before the medium stops recording a visible difference between one stop and the next.

Black-and-white and color negative films have a dynamic range of about 11 to 14 stops. Currently, 35mm digital single-lens reflex imaging sensors have a dynamic range that is closer to an average of 10 stops.

[note] This is another area where improvements to digital sensors have been dramatic. High-end professional digital cameras and scanning backs have a dynamic range of 14–15 stops, but they are very expensive.

We have learned that, within limits, the highlight values of a high-contrast subject can be pulled to within the dynamic range of film by reducing the development time. “Overdevelopment” means neglecting to do this. The term “overdevelopment” doesn’t apply to digital camera chips, but the problems caused by attempting to photograph scenes with too much contrast are just as severe as with film.

Digital images are made of pixels or individual picture elements. Texture and detail in a digital photograph is represented by a tonal difference between one pixel and its neighbors. If the dynamic range of your subject is too great, the light from the highlight areas will be too bright on your digital chip, which causes the tonal value of one pixel to “bloom,” or bleed over to adjacent pixels. This has the effect of obliterating detail in that area of the scene. The result is a bleached-out white area with no useable detail.

Figure 10.44 Blown-out highlights in a high-contrast digital image.

Histograms give us a very clear way to see this effect in terms that are especially appropriate for our Procrustean analogy.

Figure 10.45 Clipping in a high-contrast digital image.

As you can see in this illustration, both the shadow and the highlight pixel values of this image end abruptly at the extreme ends of the scale.

Subject values that fall below 0 or above 255 are said to be “clipped,” meaning that they are beyond the range of tonal levels that can be printed. Once a shadow or highlight area is clipped in a digital image, the texture and detail are simply gone and can’t be recovered—although there are special techniques that, within limits, can help with this problem. These are described in the section “Dealing with High-Contrast Subjects.”

The Adobe Camera Raw Advantage

The process of converting a digital image from its latent state, as it exists after exposure on your camera’s storage media, to the edited version in Photoshop is extremely complex, and a detailed discussion of this process is beyond the range of this book. (See Appendix W for a reference to a book that will fully explain the details of working with images in Adobe Camera Raw.)

But it’s important to have at least a general idea of what happens at this stage so you can make informed decisions about how to proceed. The first thing to understand is that all digital images start out as grayscale data after the light from the lens passes through an array of tiny colored Bayer filters positioned in front of the camera’s CMOS or CCD photosensitive detectors. (Foveon sensors work differently in this regard and are superior in many ways.) This means that the grayscale image data has to be converted into color information before it can be displayed.

Next, digital sensors measure light in very different ways than either film or our eyes do. Human vision responds to light in a distinctly nonlinear way, whereas analog-to-digital converters simply measure amounts of photons in a strictly linear manner. The bottom line is that digital images need to go through a process called tone mapping before they will look natural to our eyes. (See Appendix E for a more complete description of this issue.)

The important implication of all this is that any image corrections you make at this stage of the process are fundamental and generally much less damaging to the quality of your image than editing done in Photoshop. There are also ways that you can color-correct and otherwise interpret images during the conversion process that simply can’t be duplicated in Photoshop.

This is the reason for the next cardinal rule for digital photographers: Do as much image correction as possible (without losing vital shadow or highlight values) during the raw conversion stage before editing your image in Photoshop.

[note] Scanners can’t produce raw-formatted files that can be opened and edited using the Adobe Camera Raw utility. The reason is that scanners are creating digital images from sources that have already been through the conversion process, either as the negative or slide you are scanning or the print made from that film. This means that the .nef files produced by Nikon film scanners aren’t the same as the raw files captured by Nikon digital cameras.

Hasselblad/Imacon are able to scan film to a proprietary 3F format (.fff) that they define as being “raw” in the sense that it can be edited with special software before being converted to a tiff or JPEG file. These should not be mistaken for the nontone mapped Camera Raw files that we have been discussing.

Appreciating the importance of the raw conversion process leads directly to the question of what software you should use to do this. There are two general choices. Every manufacturer of digital cameras offers proprietary software specifically designed to convert its raw files. Nikon Capture NX and the Canon RAW Image converter are examples of this kind of software.

But here we come up against what I have discovered to be a very reliable fact: Camera manufacturers aren’t as proficient or creative at designing digital editing software as is a company that makes this its primary business. Added to this is the fact that all digital images eventually end up being edited in Adobe Photoshop in any case, so you are better off using software that is specifically designed to be compatible with this environment.

Custom Camera Profiles

With professional-level digital cameras, it’s possible to use the manufacturer’s software to apply special profiles to your images that internally modify the contrast of each photograph as you shoot. These profiles have the effect of either increasing or decreasing the image’s contrast in subtle and nondestructive ways. You can even use special software to create custom profiles that you can upload into your camera for use in unusually flat or contrasty situations.

The problem with using these custom camera profiles is that you have to remember to apply them every time you are shooting a subject with unusual contrast. Dealing with High-Contrast Subjects

An Advantage of Film

Because of the relatively limited dynamic range of current DSLR photo sensors, there are more special techniques and proposed software solutions to the problem of dealing with high-contrast subjects than almost any other issue in digital photography.

In a technologically driven culture, there’s a tendency to believe that digital processes must have advanced solutions to all photographic problems. In very many areas, this is true, but as of this writing, there is one problem for which film, especially black-and-white negative materials, retains a distinct advantage.

Because of the current limitations on what the dynamic range digital chips can record, there is nothing simple or straightforward you can do if the contrast of your subject is greater than 10–15 stops and your subject or the camera is moving—for example, if you’re photographing wind-blown trees or water flowing or people walking.

There are a number of remarkable techniques that allow digital photographers to record and print an extraordinary dynamic range of tonal values far beyond what is possible with any film, but the problem is, unless your goal is to make aesthetic use of special effects, these techniques work well only with subjects that can be shot when the camera is on a tripod and the subject is not in motion. This amounts to a fundamental difference between digital and film photography that may help you decide to stick with one or the other. If very high-contrast photography of candid, moving subjects is at the heart of your work, you may be frustrated with this limitation with digital processes.

Once again, though, it’s important not to overstate this issue. Fill flash techniques can compensate for this limitation, and there are so many other advantages to digital photography that it’s doubtful that this issue will dissuade many potential photographers.

High Dynamic Range (HDR)

The relatively limited capacity of current digital sensors to capture detail in very high-contrast subjects has inspired a number of different software developers to create applications designed to deal with this problem. What all of them have in common is that they attempt to extract image detail from a number of bracketed exposures of the same subject and combine them into one composite that appears to be a single image with a very wide tonal scale.

When HDR workflows first appeared, it was true that they generally worked best with images of subjects where there were no moving elements. More recently the utility and popularity of HDR has encouraged developers to greatly improve the range and functionality of the software so that now there are beautiful techniques you can use for subjects with elements that are in motion.

There are a number of talented artists who have produced dramatically stylized, powerful, and movingly surreal bodies of work using High Dynamic Range (HDR) methods. A great example is the work of Dan Burkholder, whose work is included in Appendix V.

Summary of Digital Photography Cardinal Rules

This chapter contains many principles, suggested techniques, and working methods that together will help you produce better photographs with your digital camera.

I highlighted the following five Cardinal Rules because they are easily overlooked, and if you do nothing else, these recommendations will greatly improve the quality of your work:

  • Always use the lowest ISO possible with digital cameras. The ability to change ISO settings from one frame to the next is an important advantage of digital photography. Most photographers are familiar with photographic grain, and some even use it to great aesthetic advantage. The same could be said of digital noise, but if reducing it is your goal, then this rule is the key.
  • Always shoot in raw format unless you are certain that you will never need the visual information stored in the raw digital file. Camera Raw files contain not only much more visual information than JPEG images, allowing for larger, higher-quality prints, but also contain useful metadata and make it possible for you to take advantage of raw conversion software. Unless image storage space is a priority and you are sure you will never need the advantages that raw files provide, this rule is always the best practice.
  • Scan or capture for your largest final print size and never interpolate if you can avoid doing so. When scanning, always determine the largest image size you ever intend to make and use that as your output size. The resolution should always be at 300 dpi if your goal is to make a high-quality inkjet print.
  • Expose for the highlights and process for the shadows. (Also known as: Expose to the Right.) This is the general guideline for optimizing digital exposures. It helps you avoid banding by reducing the need for contrast adjustments and places your shadow values as far away as possible from the darker, pixel-level-deprived end of the histogram.
  • If you are shooting raw image files, do as much image correction as possible during the raw conversion stage of the process. Because of the digital linear effect, raw images are fundamentally different from the same images after they have been converted into tone-mapped files. Raw conversion software, and especially Adobe’s Camera Raw utility and/or Adobe Lightroom, takes advantage of this process, allowing you to control factors such as image exposure and color temperature in ways that are impossible to reproduce with Photoshop.
