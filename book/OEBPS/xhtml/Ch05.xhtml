chapter five
Exposure

To understand how Zone System theory applies to exposure, let’s first consider the different types of light meters available and how they function.

Light meters come in two general types: Meters that measure what is called incident light and meters that measure reflected light.

Incident-light meters measure the light that falls on the subject from the source of light (see Figure 5.1). Incident-light meters are especially useful in lighting studios or on movie sets where the contrast can be controlled with fill-lights or reflectors. Under those conditions, your primary concern is to make sure that the amount of incident light falling on your subject does not change from one shot to another.

Figure 5.1 Incident-light reading.

Reflected-light meters measure the light that bounces off the subject to the camera and film or digital sensor (as in Figure 5.2).

As we discussed earlier, with the Zone System, we are primarily interested in measuring the contrast of a given scene. The question is: How much light is coming from one area as opposed to another? Only a meter that measures reflected light can tell us this difference.

Figure 5.2 Reflected-light reading.

There are three different types of reflected-light meters: spot meters, wide-angle meters, and built-in camera meters. Light metering applications that run on IOS or Android smartphones are similar to built-in camera meters in that the ways in which they measure reflected light depends to some extent on what the camera is seeing when it is aimed at the subject.

The first two types of reflected meters differ in terms of what is called their angle of incidence.

A wide-angle meter “sees” at an angle of approximately 30 degrees, whereas a spot meter reads at an angle of only 1 degree or less in some cases (see Figure 5.3).

Figure 5.3 Wide-angle and spot meters.

This difference in the angle of incidence is similar to the difference between looking through a straw as opposed to looking through a cone. The advantage of using a spot meter is that it allows you to measure the light from small, isolated areas very accurately at a distance. Imagine that you need to know how much light is being reflected by a model’s face. With a wide-angle meter, you would have to get very close to avoid including her hair in the reading. This is less of a problem if you can approach the subject, but if you are photographing distant mountains or performers on a stage, a wide-angle meter can make getting an accurate reflected reading very difficult, if not impossible.

The “acceptance angle” of a built-in meter varies depending on the kind of meter the camera uses and the focal length of your lens. Smartphone metering apps sometimes offer the ability to isolate small areas of the phone camera’s view. (Check the app’s manual to verify this feature.)

Thus far, I have talked about “exposing for the shadows” or “making the background Zone III” without explaining how this is actually done.

The problem of how to calculate the proper exposure becomes easy when you understand more about what all light meters are actually designed to do.

Light meters are programmed to perform two different but related functions: light measurement and exposure recommendation.

Light Measurement

The first responsibility of any light meter is to simply measure the quantity of light being reflected by the subject. Many light meters measure light using Exposure Values, or EV numbers. As you saw in Figure 4.12, EV numbers provide a very simple and intuitive way to measure amounts of light: The lighter the surface measured, the higher the number.

You also learned that because EV numbers and zones both measure amounts of light that double as they increase, you can use one to represent the other. If one surface reads EV #7 and another EV #8, you could either say that they are one EV number apart or one zone apart.

[note] Because of the conceptual simplicity of this method, many of the examples that follow will use EV numbers to illustrate the various principles being explained.

In Figure 5.4, the representation of a digital spot meter shows that the bush being read is reflecting 15 EV units of light. If the sun came out and the bush were reflecting twice as much light, the meter would read it as EV 16. Remember that unless these are EV numbers, they are arbitrary. On another meter, the same amount of light might be number 11.

Figure 5.4 Digital meter reading.

Digital light meters like the one illustrated in Figure 5.4 often have an LCD screen that simply displays its recommended exposure. In this case, the shutter speed is displayed as a number (1/30), and the red marker moves to indicate the aperture it suggests that you should use (f/11).

When in-camera light meters are in their manual mode, they measure quantities of light through “nulling” systems of one kind or another. Figure 5.5 illustrates one example. Figure 5.5A is the way an in-camera metering system would indicate 1 EV “overexposure.” Figure 5.5B indicates two stops’ “underexposure.” Figure 5.5C indicates the “correct” exposure.

Figure 5.5 In-camera meter readings.

With other in-camera systems, turning the aperture or shutter speed dial may cause a needle or red dot to move up or down until you see that the dot is in the center of a scale, or perhaps a needle is lined up with a ring.

Whichever system the meter uses, all it is doing is indicating that either more or less light is reflected by whatever is in the meter’s field of view.

Exposure Recommendations

The second thing a light meter does is to convert its light reading into an exposure that you will use to take the picture. It’s as if the meter were saying “I can see that the bush is light gray (and reflecting 15 EV units of light—the measurement), so I’m recommending that you use this combination of aperture and shutter speed (f/11 at 1/30—the exposure) to make it look that way in your print.”

Analog handheld light meters have an indicating arrow or pointer you use to line up opposite the indicated meter number. When this is done, the meter’s dial turns and matches an f/stop with a shutter speed for the “correct” exposure.

In Figure 5.6, the meter’s indicated exposure is f/11 at 1/30 of a second.

Figure 5.6 Analog meter reading with an indicator arrow.

As I mentioned previously, in-camera meters combine these two functions because to center the meter’s light measuring symbol, you have to match an aperture with a shutter speed, and this is the meter’s recommended exposure.

The Meter’s Dilemma

Unfortunately, finding the correct exposure isn’t always that simple. The problem is that light meters have no way of actually “seeing” the objects they measure. An exposure meter is simply a device that measures quantities of light, and the problem is that both a light surface and a dark surface will reflect the same amount of light if the light falling on them is changed. For example, on a bright, sunny day, a dark wall might read meter number 11, but so might a light wall on a cloudy day! To your eye, the light and dark walls are obviously very different, but to the meter, they are both meter number 11.

Think about this for a minute. “Seeing” is a complex process of visual perception that is much more than simply measuring amounts of light. For example, a piece of white paper looks white to you in both a bright light and a dimly lit room. The light meter is responding to the change in the incident light, but you can judge that the dark wall should print as Zone III and the light wall as Zone VII.

Unfortunately, the meter still has the responsibility of suggesting the correct exposure for you to use. In this example, given that the meter reads both the dark and the light walls as number 11, it obviously cannot suggest the different exposures that the two walls would require. Light meters are designed to get around this problem in a simple way: They lie!

Because the meter might read a dark wall and the white wall as the same reading if the light falling on them changes, it will simply pretend that both walls are middle gray. In this way, the exposure that it recommends can, at worst, be only half-wrong. Of course, if the wall had been gray in the first place, the meter’s recommended exposure would have been perfect.

The fact is that all light meters are programmed to give you an average gray exposure for whatever amount of light they are measuring.*

In the language of the Zone System, we would say that all light meters will automatically place any light reading on Zone V. This is very important for you to understand and remember.

This principle explains why the exposures I used in the previous chapter changed the way they did. Remember that I said that the meter reading for the white wall was 1/60 at f/11 and for the black wall was 1/60 at f/8. In both cases, the meter is suggesting an exposure that will make each wall print as middle gray. If you want to make a black wall print gray, the meter has to recommend an exposure that makes the black wall lighter. (Gray is lighter than black.) Conversely, if you want to make a white wall gray, the meter needs to recommend an exposure that makes the wall darker. (Middle gray is darker than white.)

At this point, you may be finding it hard to believe that light meters are designed to mislead you in such a dramatic way. For this reason, I would like to suggest that you try one or both of these two very simple demonstrations that will illustrate how this works.

The first is a demonstration you can do with any cellphone camera and two evenly lit surfaces—one pure white and the other pitch black.

[note] For this demonstration you should not use a light-metering app on your phone. The phone camera itself will make for the best demonstration of this effect.

All you have to do is position the camera so that all it can see is the white surface. You would expect the image on the camera’s screen to be white, but instead, as you can see in Figure 5.7, it is gray.

If you repeat this process with the camera pointed at a black surface, you will again see that the image on the screen is gray.

Figure 5.7 Phone camera rendering of white surface.

Figure 5.8 Phone camera rendering of black surface.

In both cases, the images rendered by the camera will be much closer to each other than they are to the original surfaces.

Figure 5.9 Phone camera renderings compared with black and white surfaces.

The light meters built into all cellphone cameras are very sophisticated, so this is a perfect demonstration of the way all lights meters work.

As simple and direct as this demonstration is, I strongly recommend that any reader approaching the Zone System for the first time consider working through the following, more elaborate exercise from beginning to end. You can do this with your own camera and film to illustrate how this works. This example will not only graphically illustrate the way light meters operate, but will also function as an ideal warm up for Zone System testing.

Before you begin, it is a good idea to have your light meter and shutter speeds tested for accuracy and consistency. If your technician advises you that your equipment tests within reasonable limits, proceed with this demonstration. If not, leave the equipment with the technician to be adjusted.

Exercise: How Light Meters Really Work

To do this exercise, you need five frames on any roll of film, two walls (a light one and dark one), and a reflected-light meter. A “Zone V” Neutral Gray Card from Kodak will be useful when you make a contact print of these test exposures.

Readers with digital cameras can also do this exercise using their preview window to see the results.

In choosing your test subjects, try to select walls that match your mental image of Zone IV for the dark wall and Zone VI for the light wall. This will be your first exercise in previsualization. It is very important that you find evenly textured surfaces and that you fill the frame of your camera with only those areas before you shoot. Be careful not to include your shadow in the image.

If your camera is equipped with an automatic built-in meter, you will have to either set it to its “Manual” setting or override the automatic function; otherwise, when you stop down one stop, for example, the meter will automatically change the shutter speed to one step slower to be able to maintain its Zone V exposure. Check your owner’s manual if you are not clear on how this is done with your camera. (See Chapter 7, page 84 for a discussion of how to override automatic built-in light meters.)

Exposure Plan

Frame 1 Take a careful meter reading of the dark wall and make an exposure of only that surface using the meter’s recommended f/stop and shutter speed. With built-in meters, center the dot or arrow of the meter and shoot.

Frame 2 Make an exposure of the same dark wall using an exposure that is one stop less than the first. Do this by either stopping down one f/stop from the first exposure or using one shutter speed faster. I will explain the purpose of this adjustment later in this chapter. The goal of this step of the exercise is to “underexpose” the film by one stop (Zone IV) when compared to the exposure used for frame 1.

Frame 3 Carefully meter and shoot the light wall using the meter’s recommended exposure.

[note] Be sure to take your light reading at the same angle you intend to shoot the picture and that you use the correct ASA for the film you are using. If your camera has a compensation control dial, be sure to set it at zero.

Frame 4 Photograph the light wall using an exposure that is one stop more than the meter’s recommended exposure. For frame 4 you will be overexposing the film by one stop (Zone VI) when compared to frame 3.

Frame 5 Put the lens cap on your lens and shoot one blank frame.

Process this roll of film using your standard development time and make a contact print of your five test frames. Choose an exposure time for this contact print that is the minimum amount of exposure under the enlarger that it takes to make your blank frame (frame 5) as black as it will get. To find this time, make a series of test exposures at 2-second intervals across frame 5.

The first exposure step that turns this unexposed frame as black as the paper will get represents your minimum time for maximum black. For a detailed explanation of this procedure, see Chapter 8, “Zone System Testing: Method 1.”

Your four other test frames should something look like Figure 5.10.

Figure 5.10 Zone placement test frames.

Notice that frames 1 and 3 are very similar in tone (they should approximately match your Neutral Gray Card). The reason is that the meter is blindly trying to give you the “correct” exposure by averaging the dark and light values of the walls to middle gray (Zone V).

Frames 2 and 4 should be much closer to the actual tonal values of the original walls.

[note] A valuable extension of this demonstration would be to expose two rolls of film in exactly the same way using the procedure described previously. Develop the first roll using your standard development time and the other at a time that is 20 percent longer. Contact both rolls at times that allow the first and third frames to match your Neutral Gray Card. What you will notice is that while the frame 2s on both rolls are very similar in tone, the frame 4s are noticeably different: The frame 4 (in Figure 5.10) that has been developed for 20 percent longer is noticeably lighter. This illustrates the effect of increased development on the highlight values, as described in Chapter 3, “The Control of Negative Contrast.”

The Results

What this demonstration shows is that the camera’s built-in meter is programmed to render any single subject value as an average middle gray tone, or Zone V, regardless of how dark or light it may actually be. With a few exceptions I will discuss later, all light meters are designed the same way.

What you should have learned from this demonstration is that if you were always to follow the meter’s recommendations, it would be a classic case of the blind leading the blind: You see the wall as a dark value, the meter sees it as gray, and the meter’s choice prevails. To consistently achieve the correct exposure for any subject, you need a way to tell the light meter how you want the walls to print. When you apply all that you have learned thus far, the method for doing this will seem very simple.

Exposure Detailed

The first step in the process of correctly exposing any photograph is always previsualization. Sometimes this is an explicit, intentional process; at other times it’s simply a sense that you would like the final image to faithfully render what you are seeing.

If you want to correctly expose a given subject using the Zone System, the important question you need to ask yourself in most cases will be “What part of this picture do I want to print as Zone III?” Zone III is important because it is the darkest zone that shows full texture and detail. Remember this rule: Expose for the Shadows. Take a careful look at Figure 5.11A. Imagining that this is an actual scene, which areas would you judge to be Zone III? The obvious answers are the model’s hair and the darker parts of the door on the right. For brown hair to print realistically, it has to be detailed enough to show individual strands. This automatically indicates Zone III. Blond hair is closer to Zone VII.

Place and Fall

The concept of zone “Placement” is extremely important in Zone System theory because it describes the process that will ultimately determine what aperture and shutter speed you will use for your exposure. In any photographic subject, you will always find some dark area that, in your opinion, absolutely needs adequate texture and detail. This part of your image is called the Important Shadow Area. After you have identified the Important Shadow Area, your goal in most cases will be to choose an exposure that will render that area as Zone III in the finished print. This is called “Placing the Important Shadow on Zone III” (see Figure 5.11B). Remember that Zone III is the first dark zone that has full texture and detail. Also remember that Zone III is not black.

When you decide to place a particular area of the subject on Zone III, you are saying in effect that you will not mind if any inherently darker area of the scene is black or nearly black in the finished print. Another way of putting this would be to say that after the Important Shadow has been placed on Zone III, darker areas of the subject will “Fall” on Zones 0, I, or II. The dark interior behind the model’s head in Figure 5.11A is a good example of an area that should logically fall below Zone III. You can see why the placement of Zone III is so important. Placing Important Shadow Areas of your subject too low on the Zone Scale is an excellent way to define underexposure. In this example, the model’s hair would be lost against the dark interior of the building if it were placed on a zone lower than Zone III.

Areas of the subject that are inherently lighter than the placed shadow value will, of course, fall on zones above Zone III. If the Important Highlight Areas of your subject fall above Zone VIII, they will be too white in the print, or “blocked up,” as we say. The relative positions of all the values in the subject are determined by the placement of the Important Shadow Area on the Zone Scale. If you place your shadow value on a lower zone, all the other subject values will be darker. Placing the shadow value higher on the Zone Scale will cause the rest of the subject values to be lighter. I will discuss further implications of this statement in Chapter 6, “Development.”

Once you have decided on the placement of Zone III, after you carefully meter only that area, the last step is choosing the correct f/stop and shutter speed.

There are only two points you need to remember.

With Handheld Meters

  1. Whatever light measuring system your meter uses, it will recommend an exposure that will print as Zone V unless you change it.
  2. Each meter number is equivalent to one zone and/or one f/stop or shutter speed.

With In-Camera Meters

  1. Once again, the meter’s recommended exposure (when you center the needle or dot) will place whatever the meter is reading on Zone V.
  2. Each full f/stop or shutter speed is equivalent to one zone.

An additional point to remember with in-camera meters is that they take light readings in two different general ways: automatic and manual.

All the various automatic settings (Aperture or Shutter Priority or Program) will give you a Zone V placement for whatever the meter is seeing, regardless of how you change the settings. This is why it’s crucial that you set the meter to Manual so you can control the way it renders its reading.

The first step in determining the correct exposure in this example is to carefully meter the model’s hair (previsualized as Zone III). With a spot meter, you could obtain an accurate meter reading of her hair without leaving the position of the camera. With a wide-angle or built-in meter, you would have to get close enough to the model for her hair to fill the meter’s field of view. You would also have to avoid including the shadow of your hand in the reading. As illustrated in Figure 5.12, the spot-meter reading for the model’s hair is EV 9. If you were to blindly follow the meter’s instructions, the recommended exposure for this portrait would be f/11 at 1/30 of a second at ISO 100.

Figure 5.12 Zone V placement of EV number 9 (the model’s hair). Meter recommended exposure: f/11 at 1/30 of a second.

[note] Remember that in-camera light meters don’t have meter numbers and instead translate light readings directly into recommended f/stop and shutter speed exposures. This will be discussed shortly.

Because the meter’s recommended exposure will cause her hair to print as Zone V (in this case, two zones lighter than you want it to be), if you stop down two stops from this suggested exposure (from f/11 to f/22), you will be placing the meter reading of her hair (EV 9) on Zone III, which is the way it should be printed.

Figure 5.13 illustrates how a digital meter would look with this new placement.

Figure 5.13 Zone III placement of EV number 9 (the model’s hair). Meter recommended exposure: f/22 at 1/30 of a second.

This is a key point, so let’s consider this step very carefully.

There are two factors that often make this process confusing to people new to the Zone System. The first is the logic of why the system works the way that it does.

The second confusing factor is the different methods that you have to use to adjust different light meters to make the system work.

Zone System Exposure Logic

First, after you have dutifully followed light meter suggestions for so long, it may be disconcerting to suddenly find yourself apparently underexposing from the reading the meter is providing you. But remember, the meter reading you are adjusting was based only on the specific area of the subject that you’d like to appear as Zone III (dark with full texture) in your final print. In this case, it’s the model’s hair. The meter’s recommendation for the whole scene would most likely have been very different.

Second, the reason that you stop down two stops from the meter’s recommended exposure is that, as you learned, the meter’s recommendation would print her hair as Zone V. Therefore, one stop less exposure would render her hair as Zone IV, and two stops less exposure will place her hair on Zone III, which in this case is the way you want it to look in the final print.

Of course, you could use either whole f/stops or shutter speeds to make this adjustment because zones, shutter speeds, and f/stops are equivalent. The corrected exposure in this example would then be f/22 at 1/30 of a second, or any exposure equivalent to this one.

Your concern with determining the exposure of this portrait ends here. The model’s hair and the door have been safely placed on Zone III (in this example, the meter reads them both as the same meter number), and the darker parts of the scene are falling on the lower zones where they belong.

Applying the Zone System to Different Light Meters

The second confusing factor is learning how to use various types and brands of light meters to make the system work. For example, you may be wondering why the EV reading in Figure 5.12 is 9, whereas the EV number in Figure 5.13 is 11. Here’s why.

If you decide to Place EV number 9 on Zone III, then EV number 10 will Fall on Zone IV, and EV number 11 will Fall on Zone V. And digital meter dials always display the Zone V EV number.

This concept becomes easier to understand if we were to present it on a chart similar to the one in Figure 4.11 in the preceding chapter.

Using Smartphone Light Meters with the Zone System

As of this writing, there were nine different light metering applications I was able to review, and although their interfaces differ, they all share a variety of advantages and disadvantages for use with the zone system.

The first and most obvious advantage is that they are very inexpensive and, given the ubiquity of cellphones in our lives, you are likely to have a meter available when you need one.

Another advantage that some of these applications share is that they have a semi-spot meter function that allows you to move the position of a small bracketed area within the camera’s view so that you can cause the meter to offer you a reading of that isolated area. What you will notice when you select a brighter part of your image is that that specific area will darken to approximately middle gray and the rest of the image will be correspondingly darker. And of course the reverse happens when you select a darker part of your image.

Given what we’ve learned about the way that all light meters function, these behaviors should now make perfect sense.

Most of these applications provide easy-to-read displays of the apertures and shutter speeds they are recommending and they also allow you to lock either one of these so you can watch only one or the other change.

The primary disadvantages I discovered when using all of these applications is that they are, of course, dependant on the inherent accuracy of the light metering system built into the camera itself. Smartphone cameras are improving very quickly, so it would not be surprising if cameras in the future were much better than the ones available now.

Also, although the selected metering area is small in relation to the whole view of the phone’s camera, it was still much larger than the 1 degree area of my spot meter.

Given these variables, my testing showed that the exposures recommended by these apps were one or two stops different than the exposure recommended by my spot meter.

One very useful feature is that some of these apps offer exposure compensation functions that allow you to essentially calibrate the recommended exposures so that they more closely agree with a professional, stand-alone light meter.

Taking these factors into consideration, it would then be possible to use any smartphone light metering app just the way you would with any other built-in camera meter; the process would be to:

  1. Make sure that your metering app is set to the ISO of your chosen film.
  2. Use your phone camera to frame your subject so that the camera image approximates your chosen composition.

An alternative would be to get close enough to your Important Shadow Area so that your phone camera and meter are only seeing that part of your composition.

  1. As much as possible, select the Important Shadow Area of your subject and make note of the exposure your phone meter recommends.
  2. Remembering that this recommended exposure would place the reading for that area on Zone V, place that exposure on the zone you previsualize.
  3. For example, if you previsualize that area as Zone IV you would use an exposure that is one stop less than your phone meter is recommending.

Summary

Unfortunately, explaining this procedure in detail makes it appear much more complicated than it really is. In essence, all you have really done is:

  1. Decide which areas of the scene you want to be on Zone III in the final print (in this case, the model’s hair).
  2. Meter that area and make note of the exposure that the meter is recommending, in this case, f/11 at 1/30 of a second.
  3. Stop down two stops from this recommended exposure to place the model’s hair on Zone III. The corrected exposure would be f/22 at 1/30 of a second.

If you are using an in-camera light meter, the procedure would be a little different:

  1. Set your in-camera meter to its manual mode.
  2. Previsualize your Zone III area.
  3. Get close enough to your subject so that this area fills the viewfinder.
  4. Center the needle or dot and note the exposure that the camera’s meter is recommending.
  5. Stop down two stops from this recommended meter reading to place that area on Zone III.
  6. Using this new exposure, step back and take the picture.

(See Chapter 7, page 85 for a discussion on overriding automatic built-in metering systems.)

Some people have difficulty believing that the Zone System is really that simple, or they have trouble “disobeying” their meters. If you think through this example, however, you will see how logical it is.

You now have a completely reliable way of using your light meter as a tool to give you the correct exposure according to your previsualization. If you were to previsualize a light wall as Zone VII and were unconcerned about any shadows that might be in the picture, you would simply meter the wall and open up two whole stops from the meter’s recommended exposure. Opening up one stop would place the wall on Zone VI.

Of course, for all of this to work properly, you must use the correct ASA for the film you are using. I will discuss this factor in detail in Chapter 7, “An Overview of the Zone System.” The effects of reciprocity failure, filter factors, and bellows extension are covered in Appendix R.

The problem that most beginners have with this process is previsualization. Previsualizing is an act of aesthetic judgment that offers you a wide variety of choices. There are obviously a number of effective ways to interpret any given subject. While you are learning this process, I suggest that you stick to “normal” previsualizations of your subjects. Later, when you are sure that you can consistently produce realistic results, begin experimenting with alternative ways of seeing.

What’s most important is to realize that the major advantage of the Zone System of exposure is that it’s an intentional process that allows you to place specific subject tonal values wherever you want them to be on the zone scale.

Other problems that many of us have with previsualizing black-and-white photographs are the associations we have with certain colors. Most people judge a blue object to be darker than a red one, even if both objects are actually reflecting the same amount of light. A handy device for dealing with this problem is a panchromatic viewing filter. Looking through this brown-colored filter removes most of the color from the scene and reveals the inherent contrast as the black-and-white film will see it. A Kodak Wratten Filter Number 90 will give you approximately the same effect and is usually much less expensive.
