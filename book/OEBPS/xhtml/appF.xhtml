Appendix F
Digital Light Meters and the Zone System

As you saw in the preceding chapters, analog spot meters are ideally suited for applying Zone System methods to the problems of calculating accurate exposures and determining the contrast of photographic subjects for development recommendations.

What makes the dials of these meters so useful is that they are designed to display a wide range of the EV numbers when you make a light reading so that it becomes very easy to visualize the relationships between these numbers and the exposures they correspond to. As explained in Chapter 7, it is even possible to attach Zone Scale decals to the meter dials so that you can see both the placements and the fallings of Zones in your scene at a glance.

Figure F.1 Analog meter dial with Zone placements.

Figure F.1 illustrates an analog meter dial, and as you can see, when EV 10 is placed on Zone III, EV 12 falls on Zone V, EV 13 falls on Zone VI, and so on. Also note that the equivalent exposures are also easy to see.

Figure F.2 shows the same readings with a digital meter and, although the exposure is the same (f/22 at 1/30), the placements are not visible.

Figure F.2 Digital meter dial.

And yet, as handy as these meters and dials are, there are a number of reasons why learning how to use digital light meters for the Zone System practices makes a lot of sense.

For one thing, analog meters like the classic Pentax Spotmeter V are no longer readily available. Photographers these days are much more likely to have multipurpose digital meters like the ones made by Sekonic and Gossen, among others.

Another major advantage of digital meters is their versatility. Meters like the Sekonic Digitalmaster L-758DR function as both spot and incident, ambient and flash meters, and they come with many useful features that are designed to address a wide range of different photographic applications.

This extreme versatility also tends to make these meters complicated to master, so a good approach would be to carefully understand how you need the meter to function in specific situations and then ignore all of the other various features that are not necessary for your work.

To illustrate this point, I would like to illustrate how one specific and popular professional meter applies to the kinds of Zone System–related problems we have been learning.

The meter I’ll use is the previously mentioned Sekonic Digitalmaster L-758DR. If you own this meter, please refer to the owner’s manual for detailed instructions on how to perform some of these functions. If you follow along with this example, you will see that most digital light meters can be made to work in ways similar to these examples.

Digital Light Meter Settings

The first challenge owners of digital spot meters may have is setting them up so that they display EV numbers. As we have seen, using EV numbers to read reflected subject areas makes the process of measuring contrast very easy. Unfortunately, by default, many brands of digital meters display only f/stop reading and shutter speeds unless you do something special to make EV readings visible.

For example, with the Sekonic L-758DR, the “EV Mode” is listed in the user’s manual as “Custom setting no.5 and Item no.1.” This means that you have to carefully follow the manual’s instructions to activate the Custom setting mode and then choose the numbers just listed.

With older models of Sekonic meters, if you remove the battery cover, you will see a row of tiny white dual in-line package (DIP) switches that allow you to turn on the EV mode. (Consult your meter’s manual for detailed instructions for how to do this.)

The Zone System and Digital Meters

In the preceding chapters, we learned that there are four key steps to working with the Zone System to create properly exposed and developed negatives:

  1. Previsualizing.
  2. Placing your Important Shadow on the proper Zone.
  3. Measuring the contrast of your subject using Zones.
  4. Determining the correct exposure.

The following sections show how you use a digital spot meter to perform these steps.

Previsualization, Placement, and Contrast Measurement

Imagine that Figure F.5 is a subject you are planning to photograph. The figure shows a reasonable way to previsualize this photograph.

Figure F.5 Zone Placements.

With your digital spot meter set to EV mode, you could easily take meter readings of all these key areas and enter them on a simple Zone Scale form like the one illustrated here.

[note] Digital meters offer alternative ways of comparing shadow and highlight readings. For example, with the Sekonic meter we’re using for this demonstration, you can meter shadow and highlight areas and use the Memory function to place markers on the f/stop numbers that correspond to each. The idea is that with these readings displayed on the dial, you could either count the number of stops between these two or use the Average Button to display an exposure between these two. This would give you an exposure that might be approximately correct, but the Zone System method is much more precise and flexible.

As you can see, charted in this way, the range of contrast of this subject is clear and, because the Important Highlight is falling on the proper Zone, this negative would require Normal Development. (See Chapter 6 for a more detailed explanation of this process.)

Exposure Determination

We learned in Chapter 5 that the first step in the process of determining the correct exposure would be to carefully meter the area you previsualize as Zone III in your subject.

Your meter would look like Figure F.6 after making this reading.

Figure F.6 Zone V exposure recommendation (1/30th @ f/5.6) for area previsualized as Zone III.

When you keep in mind that, at this point in the process, your meter is recommending a Zone V placement for this Important Shadow area, your next step would be to simply make note of the meter’s recommended Zone V exposure for this area and stop down to the proper exposure of 1/30 at f/11 and then take the picture.

Although this method will work, the disadvantage is that it would require you to remember to make the necessary two-stop adjustment, and it is not obvious what the equivalent exposures to the proper placement would be if you needed either a different shutter speed or f/stop.

Using the Sekonic Digitalmaster L-758DR meter as an example, the following procedure shows a slightly more complicated but functional way to do this:

  1. Make sure that the meter’s EV reading for the Zone III area is visible on the meter’s dial.

    Figure F.7 Zone V exposure recommendation (1/30th @ f/5.6) for area previsualized as Zone III.

  2. Enter the meter’s Exposure Compensation Mode by holding down both the ISO1 and ISO2 buttons at the same time. Note that you’ll probably have to lay the meter on a table to do this effectively. This is the way the meter’s dial will look:

  3. Turn the meter’s jog wheel until you see the number +2 in the window. This indicates that you are making a two-stop adjustment to the meter’s Zone V recommended exposure.

  4. As soon as you let go of the two ISO buttons, the meter will revert to the EV mode and you will notice that the meter’s EV reading is two EV numbers higher and that the recommended exposure is two stops darker.

This is the correct Zone III placement of your original meter reading of the area previsualized as your Important Shadow area.

Figure F.8 Zone III exposure recommendation (1/30th @ f/11) for area previsualized as Zone III.

As discussed in Chapter 5, the logic of what the meter is now displaying is as follows: If EV number 6 is placed on Zone III, then EV number 8 falls on Zone V.

If the meter’s Zone V placement of EV 6 was 1/30th at f/5.6, then the corrected Zone III placement of that reading would be two stops less exposure, or 1/30th at f/11.

If this is not clear, review Chapter 5.

The key advantage of using this method is that, when the meter is displaying the correct Zone III placement, you can use the meter’s jog wheel to change the recommended exposure to any of its equivalents and be sure that all of them are correct.

One very important point to note is that, with this exposure compensation adjustment programmed into your meter, it will always automatically place a reading it makes two stops darker than it normally would. What you would now have is a Zone III placing digital light meter. This could make the process of determining Zone III placements very easy, but you would always have to remember only to take meter readings of areas you previsualize as Zone III. If you were to forget and try to use this meter in a normal way, your exposures would be consistently underexposed. For this reason, it might be a better idea to set it back to Exposure Compensation 0 after each time you use the meter.
