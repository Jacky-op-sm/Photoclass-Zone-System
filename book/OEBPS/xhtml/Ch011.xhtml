About Bill Brandt’s Photograph

The photograph that serves as the key example of this chapter is titled “Nude, Campden Hill, London, April 1949,” a masterpiece of tableau photography by an artist who is an inspirational favorite of mine and also of Ansel Adams. I know this because the last original print I saw of this photograph was on Ansel’s wall in 1983, a year before he died.

Much more of Bill Brandt’s work can be seen at the website of his Archive at www.billbrandt.com/. Reproductions of this and limited edition fine prints can be purchased from his estate.

Although Bill Brandt did not use the Zone System for the creation of his image, the lighting techniques he employed were highly sophisticated and would represent a considerable challenge for anyone attempting to use controlled lights to establish mood and aesthetic emphasis in a studio setting. This is precisely the problem even skilled professionals encounter in commercial studios in all parts of the world.

My re-creation of his work is titled “Homage to Bill Brandt.” As you will see from your viewing of Bill Brandt’s vast body of historic and classic work, he was a visionary master. My photograph is a humble tribute to his influence.

Introduction

Ever since the beginnings of photography in 1839, photo-image makers have had, in very general terms, two significantly different ways to create meaningful pictures.

The first was something radically new at the time. Let’s call it the Mode of Photographic Discovery. For photographers working in this way, relevant meanings were “out there” in the world to be discovered through a process of investigation, perhaps travel, and then careful and accurate rendition. In this broad conceptual sense, both landscape and documentary photographers can be described as working within this mode of expression, and until that moment, with the possible exception of descriptive writing, artists had no other ways to extract meanings directly from the visual world. Along with the illusion of objectivity, this was a breakthrough.

In contrast, again from the very beginning, there were photographers who worked within the Mode of Photographic Fabrication. This means projecting internal images and fantasies onto the world by creating illusionistic worlds in studios, much in the way a painter or drawing artist does in a free play of imagination.

Portrait photography and the work of artists such as Sally Mann, Larry Sultan, and Philip-Lorca diCorcia, among many others, occupies a middle ground between these two general headings, but let’s consider what the Zone System has to offer each of these approaches.

We have seen how the Zone System applies to photographers working in the Discovery mode: It’s now possible to look at the tonal value of any part of a subject and know precisely how to translate that into an exposure and development time that will create a negative or digital file that is very easy to print.

This chapter explains how the Zone System can be a very practical tool for working with complex lighting setups in photographic studios. Along the way, you will learn some important basics about flash meters, lighting principles, and how the Zone System can function as a tool for creative visualization in the studio.

One issue that is not often considered is the way Discovery photographers are sometimes constrained by the inclination to simply record the world just as it is. One of the key advantages of working with the Zone System is that it makes it very easy for artists to photograph the world as they imagine it, rather than as the way it appears to be. The goal is for photography to become an open creative process with many possible outcomes. An example of this is the image of a beach in Marin County, California, that I used in Chapter 4, “The Zone.”

Parts A and B of Figure 11.1 are photographs of exactly the same stretch of beach in Northern California, although many years apart. The image on the left is the way it actually looks, and the one on the right is my interpretation of it using Zone System techniques of previsualization and exaggerated exposure and development.

If alternative or surrealistic interpretations of the world are your goal, it can sometimes be difficult to see past the natural state of things in front of your lens. But consider how this issue applies to photographers working through a process of Fabrication.

Figure 11.1 Two renderings of Muir Beach.

Directorial, or Fabricative, photographers are free to create almost anything they can imagine! And, of course, the range of artists and approaches under this heading is vast. From masters such as Arthur Tress and Jerry Uelsmann who create fantasies from dreams, to Irving Penn and Gregory Crewdson who redefined what it means to work in a studio, Fabricative artists start with acts of imagination and build their artworks from scratch using elements that they both find and create.

What the Zone System can do is provide photographers with a workflow that simplifies the process of previsualizing and manifesting creative visions in studio settings.

Digital Workflow and the Power of the Zone System

Modern digital photographic studios are often equipped with high-resolution digital backs that are tethered to computers and monitors that allow for instant visual feedback as adjustments to composition and lighting are made. In a very real sense, this might seem to eliminate the need for a process like the Zone System. And, in fact, many professional photographers working in studios no longer need or even own light meters for most of their work.

There are still, of course, many studio fine art and commercial photographers who continue to shoot film or are using untethered systems that would make workflows like the one described here very practical. And even the most experienced digital photographers sometimes are confronted with novel situations in which a light meter becomes a very handy tool.

However, the real power and utility of the Zone System in a studio setting is that it allows photographers to visualize and translate sometimes confusing or obscure lighting concepts and treatments into a precise and intuitive visual vocabulary that can be easily converted into light meter readings and exposures.

For example, photographers familiar with the Zone System know that normal Caucasian skin tones fall naturally within the range of Zone VI. Instead of saying, “I’d like her skin to be somewhat lighter than normal” or “Let’s make the ratio of her skin to the background 2 to 1,” a Zone System–trained photographer could instinctively say, “I want her skin to be Zone VII or Zone VIII.” This statement not only has specific aesthetic implications and meanings, it also automatically translates into specific meter readings the photographer can use to position lights and expose the image.

Even saying “Make her skin one stop brighter” is more ambiguous than “Zone VI to Zone VII” because, in the language of the Zone System, these zones refer to specific tonalities that the photographer can see in his or her mind. Plus, we’ve learned that this change from one Zone to the next automatically means an increase of one stop and a ratio of 2 to 1.

This same process applies to visualizing, positioning lights, and exposing every part of a complex studio-lit tableau.

The Lighting Studio as a Creative Environment

The concept of a “studio” is as fundamental to photographers as it is to a painter or sculptural artist. Working in a studio embodies the seemingly contradictory factors of control and freedom. The reason you are in a studio is so that you have the ability to control all of the elements that comprise the creative process. The subjects, props, background, and lighting are all things you can position and control as if the whole setting were a blank canvas for you to work with. In this sense, the studio becomes a metaphor for the interior of your imagination. The closed door allows you to manifest anything you can imagine and have the skills to create.

Hot Lights Versus Strobes

Photographers have many choices for how to equip and work with lights in a studio, and most of the basic concepts you will learn in this section apply as much to using continuous or “hot” lights as they do to using strobes. The advantage of using hot lights is that they allow you to see in advance precisely what the film or digital back will see when the image is exposed. There are, however, some inherent disadvantages to working with continuous lighting sources:

  1. They are called hot lights for a very good reason. Tungsten light sources especially become so hot when they are left on that they can easily ignite anything flammable in their proximity, and they can be very uncomfortable to sit under for long periods of time.

  2. By far the least expensive and most practical sources of continuous lighting in a photographic studio are tungsten lights, but they are not balanced for daylight as are the sensors in digital cameras and backs. You can convert the color temperatures of tungsten lights to daylight through software relatively easily if you’re shooting in camera raw format, but shooting in daylight from the beginning simplifies things.

    Although daylight-balanced continuous lighting sources are readily available, they can be very bulky and expensive, such as Hydrargyrum medium-arc iodide (HMI) units.

  3. Although tungsten lights appear very bright to our eyes, they, in fact, produce far less light than strobe units, meaning that you are restricted to relatively wide apertures and narrow depth of field.

For all these reasons, this chapter will concentrate on the uses of strobe lighting sources in the studio.

Understanding Flash Meters

Applying Zone System concepts to the process of working with strobe lights in the studio means first understanding how digital flash meters work and then how to translate what they do into the visual language you’ve learned in the preceding chapters of this book.

For the purposes of illustrations in this chapter, I will be using diagrams based on the Sekonic L-758DR DigitalMaster Flash Meter as my model. There are many very good flash meters on the market, but the Sekonic is extremely versatile because it functions as an incident, spot, ambient, and strobe meter all in one. All the concepts I’ll be describing will be easy to translate into applications for meters from other manufacturers.

There are three basic concepts that studio photographers need to understand to make best use of flash meters and the ways they can be used with the Zone System.

1 Only the Aperture Matters

This statement is an exaggeration, but it makes an important point that can be easily understood if you imagine that you are a tiny person, standing inside a camera in a dark lighting studio.

Let’s say that the lens aperture is set to f/11 and the shutter speed to 1/60th of a second. The duration of typical studio strobe flashes can be as short as a few milliseconds, so when the shutter is released, here’s what you would see:

  • First, the shutter opens, but because the studio is dark, you see nothing.
  • Then, while the shutter is still open, the strobe fires, and for a very brief time, you see the whole setup in front of the camera.
  • After the strobe light disappears, the room is again dark.
  • Finally, the shutter finishes its duration and closes.

The fact that the shutter was actually open for 1/60th of a second did not produce any effect on this exposure.

Because the flash duration is so much faster than any shutter, the shutter speed is usually irrelevant for calculating exposures when working in the studio. Other than the strobe burst, the only light source that could have made an impression on the film or digital sensor would have been a light that had been visible the whole time of this exposure. This is why slow shutter speeds become a factor only when you’re shooting scenes with light bulbs, windows, or candles that are lit for the whole duration of your shot, or when you are using strobes as a supplemental light source when shooting outdoors.

Figure 11.2 Typical digital flash meter dial.

Notice that when you look at the dial of a typical digital flash meter, it appears to give special prominence to the aperture rather than the shutter speed.

Of course, the absolute value of this reading also depends on the ISO rating of your film or digital back.

2 Flash Apertures and Distance

So far in this book, I have given great emphasis to the value of using reflected-light meter readings for working with the Zone System. The reason is that our concern has been primarily to know how much light is being reflected from various areas of the scenes we’ve been photographing. Understanding how to place these readings where we want them to be on the Zone Scale is the essence of the Zone System of exposure.

But, at this point, it’s important to note that incident-light meters are extremely useful in studio settings because they measure amounts of light that are falling on various subjects on your set.

Figure 11.3 Spherical diffuser and incident-light meter illustration.

Incident-light meters have sensors that are under what are called spherical diffusers that look like half of a small ping-pong ball. The diffuser simulates the effect of light falling onto a three- dimensional object, and the meter averages this combination of light and dark to render a Zone V exposure reading.

We saw this illustrated in Chapter 5, Figure 5.1.

This diagram was useful for making the distinction between incident- and reflected-light meters, but now I can point out something that doesn’t make sense in this drawing: Although I show beams of light that radiate outward from the sun, I also show oddly parallel beams of light falling on the meter and shrub. The fact is that, other than laser light, all other forms of light spread away from light sources and decrease in brightness in a very predictable way that’s illustrated in Figure 11.4.

Figure 11.4 The Inverse Square Law and apertures.

The Inverse Square Law requires that, in order to keep the exposure the same, every time you double the distance between the light source and the flash meter, you have to compensate for the loss of illumination by increasing the exposure by two stops.

One key element of this explanation is the part about keeping the exposure the same. Another way of expressing this would be to say that, every time you move a light meter farther away from a light source, the brightness of the total amount of light falling on a given area decreases, and therefore the meter asks for a wider aperture to keep the exposure the same as before.

Keep this concept in mind.

3 Flash Meter Readings, Zones, and the Key Aperture

The reason that light meters ask for more exposure the farther they are from a light source has to do with the now-familiar fact that all light meters are designed to recommend exposures that render whatever is being measured as a middle gray or Zone V value.

We have also learned that, in lighting studios, flash meters are designed to offer readings in terms of an aperture that corresponds to the distance it is from the light source: the closer the light, the smaller that aperture the meter will recommend.

As I mentioned earlier, all flash meters primarily read out with aperture numbers because the speed of the strobe burst is so much faster than the shutter speed built into the camera.

But, of course, the essential question for taking any photograph is, “What specific aperture should I use for this image?”

When you’re taking any photograph, the issue of aperture choice is always only one part of a two-part question. Normally, you would be asking:

  1. What aperture should I use for the depth of field I need?

    The second part of this exposure question is:

  2. What shutter speed should I use that corresponds to this aperture to give me the proper exposure?

But, as we’ve just seen, in studio photography this second question needs to be replaced with a different question related to the intimate relationship between apertures and distance in lighting studios. We will get to that question in a bit.

First, let’s consider question #1. As always, the proper aperture for a given image depends entirely on the amount of depth of field required for the key areas of the image to be sharp.

One complicating factor when working in a studio is that it may be difficult to preview an aperture’s depth of field because the image on the ground glass simply becomes too dark to see which areas are sharp and which are not.

One handy trick that I often use when shooting in the studio is to create what I call a “magic wand” out of a small ball of crumpled aluminum foil on the end of a long stick.

Figure 11.5 Studio lighting magic wand.

Because the crumpled silver ball has many shiny, highly reflective surfaces, it is easy to see specular reflections bouncing off it when a bright studio focusing light is positioned near it anywhere in the set. Even with the lens stopped far down on the scale, the sparkles from the wand make it clear if a given area of the subject is sufficiently sharp.

Using your magic wand, you can easily choose which aperture will be ideal for a given photograph. From now on, we’ll call this your Key Aperture, and it’s important to remember that this is the aperture that your camera lens is set to and that you are actually using to take the picture.

For the sake of this example, let’s say that the Key Aperture that we are using to take a given image is f/11. To illustrate how lighting distance plays an important role in the second exposure question in the studio, let’s begin with the most simple example we can imagine. Shortly, we will see how this process applies to a much more complex lighting problem.

Suppose you were commissioned to produce a copy image of what we’ll call “The Zone Flag.”

Figure 11.6 The Zone Flag.

Treating this as you would any piece of flat artwork you were shooting, you would have two goals, and both would determine how you positioned and adjusted your lights. Your first goal is to make sure that the lighting is even from one corner of the flag to the other. Most likely, you would do this by setting up two broad light sources and positioning them at equal distances on either side of the flag at 45-degree angles. (The trick for doing this is to have your assistant hold a pencil in the middle and move the lights until the shadows on each side are the same length.)

Your second goal is to make sure that your reproduction of this flag perfectly matches the tonal values of the original. It’s one thing to know that your lights should be at equal distances from your subject, but the important question is: Exactly how far away should my lights be from my subject?

This is the second exposure question when working in a lighting studio.

Achieving Normal Contrast in the Lighting Studio

While you were learning the Zone System in the earlier chapters of this book, your goal was to understand how to manipulate the contrast of either your negative or your digital file so that the variable contrast of your subject would match the contrast or dynamic range of your photographic or inkjet prints.

The lighting studio presents you with very different goals:

  • The art of working in a lighting studio is having an effective aesthetic vision of how best to fabricate and light your subject.
  • The craft of working in a lighting studio is knowing how to control the placement of lights and shadows on your subject so that no further manipulation is necessary.

The whole point of using lights in a dark room is control of subject contrast so that reproduction of the negative or digital file is an easy and straightforward process.

If your readings indicate that a part of your subject is too dark, you could move your lights a bit closer. If another part is too bright, you could move that light farther away, or, as is often the case, partially blocking the light will create the necessary result.

[note] So far in this discussion, I’ve been emphasizing the role of distance in determining the brightness and exposure in the lighting studio. But strobe power packs offer you another effective way to control the brightness and exposure of strobes: the watt-second settings on the power pack itself. (See Appendix B for more information about this factor.)

Understanding the Lighting Studio Zone Chart

It is important to understand the logic of how this chart is filled in.

Figure 11.10 Zone Flag and Studio Zone Chart.

The Key Aperture is the aperture your lens is actually set to because this gives you the amount of depth of field you need. (Keep in mind that often wide apertures and very shallow depth of field can be aesthetically very effective.)

Once the Key Aperture is filled into this chart, it then becomes very easy to fill in all the other respective full apertures so that you can see where they fall on the Zone Scale.

Using our Zone Flag example: The reason that f/11, the Key Aperture, is entered in the Zone V place on the chart is that it is the flash spot meter reading we got from the gray card after we repositioned the lights. If, again with a flash spot meter, we were then to take a reading of any other part of the flag, we would immediately know how light or dark that area would be in the resulting photograph. With the Key Aperture f/11, placed on Zone V, the chart shows that f/5.6 falls on Zone III and f/22 falls on Zone VII.

Here is an explanation for why this is true: Say you spot meter an area, and the reading is f/5.6. Looking at our scale in Figure 11.10, we can see that this area will end up being Zone III (textured dark) in the resulting image. Why Zone III? What’s happening is that the spot meter is asking for f/5.6 to make the area being measured into a Zone V value.

The meter is asking for f/5.6, as shown here.

However, the camera is set to f/11, as shown here.

Because the camera is set to an aperture that is two stops smaller than the meter is asking for, that area will end up being rendered two stops darker than Zone V; in other words, it will be Zone III in the resulting image. This same reasoning explains the placement of all the apertures on the chart in Figure 11.10.

This method and chart provide a very simple way to know in advance exactly how light or dark any part of your subject will eventually be. All you have to do is meter that area with your spot flash meter and see where the resulting aperture reading falls on the Zone Chart.

This also translates into a simple rule you can use to understand how spot and incident flash meters work:

  • When they read out with higher apertures, the light being measured is brighter.
  • When they read out with lower apertures, the light being measured is darker.

Incident Meters Never Lie

I hope that the preceding explanation makes this process of spot flash metering and moving your lights seem like a very logical and practical system, and in complex lighting situations or when digital backs with tethered monitors are not available, this may be true.

But, in practice, there is actually a much more direct and simple way to resolve lighting problems like determining the correct exposure of our Zone Flag or, for that matter, any flat art or simple table top studio lighting setup: Use an incident flash meter and do exactly what it tells you to do.

The Zone V gray card in the middle of this flag makes this a very easy subject to work with using either a spot or incident flash meter. With an incident meter, you can simply take a meter reading of the light falling on the gray card and use its reading as the correct exposure for a Zone V placement. The advantage of using a spot meter is that it’s the only way to measure the tonal values of different parts of your scene so you can make creative adjustments as described before.

Often all you want is for your final print to look as much like your original as possible. If that’s the case, not being able to read the individual parts is less important. As described previously, incident meters average the light falling on the spherical diffuser to an exposure that renders that amount of light to Zone V.

Looking again at our Zone Flag, here’s how you might diagram the way you would use an incident meter to not only determine the correct exposure for the gray card in the center, but also to make sure that the lighting is even.

Figure 11.11 The Zone Flag with incident readings.

Unlike Figure 11.8 where the spot meter readings were different for different parts of the flag, in this case, the incident meter is completely oblivious to what’s behind it. All it can do is measure that light falling on the flag, and this makes it very easy to tell whether one area is lit more than another.

The other implication of this is important to understand, although it can seem counterintuitive at first: An incident meter reading that renders a gray card in a given setup as gray, will also render a white surface in that scene as white and a black surface as black. Just looking at Figure 11.11 makes this concept clear. If the gray card in the image is correctly exposed, all of the other values in that same image will be also.

What this means is that you always have two choices if all you want to do is render things in your lighting studio setup just as they are:

  1. You could place a gray card in the scene so that the same amount of light is falling on it as is on the subjects. Then you could take a careful spot meter reading of the card and use that exposure to take the picture. If you need more depth of field than the recommended aperture allows (you can use your magic wand to check for this), all you would have to do is move your lights in closer or increase the output of your power pack until the meter reads the proper aperture.
  2. Alternatively, you could simply place an incident meter in the scene so that the same amount and pattern of light is falling on the spherical diffuser as is on the scene and use that to take the picture. Again, the position or output of your lights may need to be adjusted if you need more or less depth of field. Incident-light meters never lie.

Given what you have just learned, it might seem as if the solution to all studio lighting problems is simply to obey what incident-light meters recommend. The problem is that incident meters are less useful when you’re dealing with more complicated lighting situations. They are often very helpful for getting started, but if, for example, you need to know whether one area is inherently darker than another and therefore needs more light, you would need a more rigorous way of working. This is where the Zone System and the things that we have learned in the previous sections can be very useful.

Some Comments Regarding Equipment and Workflow Equipment

Bill Brandt used a customized camera and continuous light sources to create his famous series of nude tableaus, and he certainly did not use the Zone System. Contemporary studio-based photographers still utilize continuous, or “hot,” lights for special purposes, but for a number of good reasons, they are much more likely to use electronic strobes as light sources for their work.

One reason is that strobe lights are considerably brighter than hot lights, allowing for the use of much smaller apertures and greater depth of field when that is required. In this case, that was essential because we were not able to stop down to the extremely small f/stops that Brandt used for this series.

[Step 3] Previsualizing Tonal Values

The next important step for any serious studio lighting project is to translate the tonal values of the image into the key zones we would be using to refine both the specific placement and intensity of the lights.

It is always a good idea to begin studio-based photographs with a rough drawing to help you organize your composition and resolve any conceptual problems before you begin styling and shooting. As discussed earlier, this is the step in the process that becomes a challenge to your creative imagination. You could simply attempt to reproduce the objects on the set just as your eyes see them. Or, you could push the representation of your subject to whatever degree of abstraction or exaggeration that you believe most effectively conveys your expression.

The advantage of thinking in the language of zones at this stage is that there is no limit to what you can imagine. A surface that is Zone III to your eye could become Zone 0 on your drawing. Recording the zones on paper becomes an act of visualization that amounts to a precise blueprint for upcoming steps in the process.

[Step 6] Determining the Meter Readings

To say that the next step was setting up the lights so that we derived meter readings that corresponded to the Studio Zone Chart would be to dramatically understate a very complex process. Some of this step involved choosing materials that would naturally facilitate the placements we needed. For example:

  • Using black paper for the floor and wall behind the model’s body.
  • Painting the walls and molding so that they matched the treatment we wanted.
  • Of course, choosing a white sheet for the bed.

But the most important part of this step in the process was determining which lights of what size needed to be placed at specific distances to create the effect we needed. Having a very good predetermined sense of what we were attempting to re-create in terms of zones was extremely helpful, but we also needed to test different exposures via the digital camera we were using.

Both incident and spot meter readings are often necessary for complex shots like this. As we learned previously, incident meters can be trusted to give you a starting point exposure for parts of the subject that are nearby.

Spot meter readings are helpful for measuring the brightness of areas that are smaller, farther away, or otherwise not easy to reach. In every case, a combination of adjusting the output of the power pack and moving the lights back and forth eventually resulted in the balance of readings that we needed.

Summary of Preceding Steps

Before moving on to the details of how the shooting process for this specific image unfolded, let’s summarize what we have done so far.

We have learned that three factors are central to this process:

  • Distance: First between light sources and the subject. The importance of distance is derived from the Inverse Square Law and must be accounted for so that meter readings and exposure make sense.
  • A Key Aperture: This is specifically related to amounts of depth of field and the degree of sharpness your photograph needs to be effective. Sometimes very shallow depth of field is by far the most evocative way to see an image. At other times, overall sharpness is essential. Either way, your Key Aperture embodies this aesthetic decision.
  • Zones: The zones connect what you can see and imagine to images you can easily print.

All these factors can be integrated into a seamless whole in the process of creating a lighting studio photographic statement.

There are three steps in the conceptual, formal, and strictly technical process of creating a complex studio lighting project that can come together in the final image.

A The Vision

Every studio photograph is to some extent or another a visualized story. Even the most prosaic-seeming image of an empty glass is saying something about what came before and what might come after the slice of time we are seeing. In the example cited here, the narrative tableau is one derived from the inspiration of a great artist, Bill Brandt. Your challenge is to make your visions of photographic meaning as evocative as you can.

B The Vision Translated into Zones

The recording of your initial vision of the image into Zones is a powerful step. Zones are visual tonal entities, but at the same time, they embody specific technical factors that are functional for the creation of any photographic image you choose to make. There are no limits to what you can visualize in your imagination, but, when those mental images are translated into zones, you can be sure that your vision will be easy to render.

C The Translation of Zones into Meter Readings

Having a map of specific meter readings, derived from the zones, means that you are ready to begin the process of actually taking the photograph.

The Shooting Process

Preparing

Careful and detailed preparation is the key to any successful studio shoot, but this is especially important when you’re working with models. The ideal would be to have the set so well prepared that the model could simply step into the frame to be photographed. In real life, things are seldom this simple.

In this case, Laura and I worked out all the basics of set creation and lighting as described previously, but it still took quite a while for me to internalize the language I would use to direct our model so that the precise combination of posture and temperament could be fully present and resolved at the moment I released the shutter.

Separating the Background

One key element of the lighting design was something very common for elaborate in-studio photography setups: foreground and background isolation.

It is often essential that the light that fully illuminates the key element nearest to the camera does not cast any light on elements that are farther away. This allows you to essentially combine two completely different lighting setups into one seamless image. And, as you will see, for the creation of this image, we, in effect, were working with three combined lighting arrangements in this photograph.

We have seen how the Inverse Square Law can work very effectively to ensure this effect. Consider what happens when one light is falling on the model.

As you can see in Figure 11.16, the spot flash meter reading of her shoulder is asking for f/32.

The meter is of course trying to render her skin as Zone V with this recommended aperture.

In this image, the lens is set to our Key Aperture of f/16.

This is why her skin shows up as two zones brighter than Zone V: Zone VII.

But consider what aperture would have been required for this one light to render the background as visible: The bed is at least 10 feet away from the model so that a meter reading from this one light at that distance might have been f/2.8.

With only one light falling on the model, your meter reading of the bed in the background is asking for f/2.8.

But instead your Key Aperture is f/16, as shown here.

In other words, if we were to only have used this one light, the figure would be brightly lit and the background would be completely black! This is why we needed two other lights to illuminate the rest of the “room.”

Lighting the “Room”

So far, imagine that the model is powerfully lit by one strobe head with a grid over its face that’s positioned very close to her shoulder.

[note] A grid is a honeycombed attachment to the flash head that has the effect of channeling the light into narrow bands, making the light very focused and hard. (See Appendix B for more information about hard versus soft lighting effects.)

The model is lit, but the rest of the studio is very dark. In Zone System terms, the goal of the rest of this step would be to bring the brightness of the bed up from Zone 0 to Zone VIII. This required two additional lights: one a normal, broader light and one a small, harder light. The specific position of these lights was determined by carefully evaluating the needed exposure and shadow patterns.

Achieving the Final Lighting Effect

At this point, you can imagine that there are at least two different lighting treatments combined into the single image that resulted: one for the model and another for the bed and walls. But, if you look carefully, you can see that there is one other essential element of the lighting of this tableau: the candle.

Earlier in this chapter, I used the example of the way a long shutter speed would look from the point of view of a tiny being inside a camera during a strobe photographic exposure. To review:

  • The room is dark.
  • The shutter opens, but because the room is pitch black, you still see nothing.
  • The strobe fires and, for very brief moment you can see all that is visible in the room.
  • The shutter closes, and the exposure is over.

I used this thought experiment to illustrate why shutter speeds are generally not a factor for determining effective exposures when you’re shooting with electronic strobes. But recall that I also cited instances when anything generating light during the whole of the long shutter exposure would, in fact, make an impression on the film or digital sensor.

This is a good moment to remind you that in Latin the word “camera” means “chamber” or “room.”

What Laura, the model, and I experienced during this shoot was essentially equivalent to being in the middle of that thought experiment.

With the lights on, I carefully positioned the model and made sure that the lens was focused properly and that the aperture was set to f/16.

  • The candle was lit.
  • The lights were turned off so that, except for the dim light from the candle, the studio was completely dark.
  • I asked the model to prepare herself for the exposure and then opened the shutter.
  • With the shutter open, the digital sensor could see only the candle and began recording its glow.
  • The strobe fired, and for very brief moment, we could see the model and the whole set.
  • The shutter remained open for a total of 15 seconds.
  • The shutter closed, and the exposure was over.

The warm glow of light on the wall and, most especially, on the model’s body is the result of this procedure. This is how “Homage to Bill Brandt” was created.
